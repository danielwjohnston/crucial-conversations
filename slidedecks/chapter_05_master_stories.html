How to Control Your Emotions and Assumptions

"The story you tell yourself about what happened is often more powerful than what actually happened."

The Power of Stories

Our emotions aren't driven by facts themselves, but by the stories we tell ourselves about those facts.

Two people can witness the exact same event and have completely different emotional responses based on their stories.

The key to emotional control isn't suppressing feelings—it's mastering the stories that create them.

Think About It:

When was the last time you had a strong emotional reaction? What story were you telling yourself?

Spot the Story by Watching for "Hot Words"

To avoid confusing story with fact, watch for hot terms. These are words that express judgments and attributions.

Story Words (Hot Terms):

  • "She scowled at me"
  • "He made a sarcastic comment"
  • "She's being defensive"
  • "He's so arrogant"
  • "They're controlling"

These words express judgments, not observations. They are story, not fact.

Story vs. Fact: The Difference

Story (Hot Words):

"She scowled at me"

"He made a sarcastic comment"

"Lewis was controlling and didn't respect her"

Fact (Observable Behavior):

"Her eyes pinched shut and her lips tightened"

"He said 'That's a brilliant idea' with a smirk"

"He talked a lot and met with the boss one-on-one"

Notice how much less volatile the factual descriptions are. They allow for multiple interpretations.

The Three Stories We Tell

Victim Story

"I'm the innocent victim in this situation. Others are doing this TO me."

Creates: Helplessness, resentment, powerlessness

Villain Story

"They are the bad guys. They have malicious intent."

Creates: Anger, judgment, self-righteousness

Helpless Story

"There's nothing I can do. I'm trapped."

Creates: Resignation, anxiety, inaction

Why We Tell These Stories

These stories serve a purpose—they help us feel better about ourselves:

  • Victim stories make us feel morally superior
  • Villain stories justify our negative emotions
  • Helpless stories excuse us from taking action

The Problem:

While these stories might make us feel better temporarily, they keep us stuck and prevent real problem-solving.

The first step to emotional control is recognizing when you're telling yourself these stories.

How to Master Your Stories

Step 1: Separate Facts from Story

Look for hot words. What actually happened vs. what you're telling yourself about it?

Step 2: Look for Your Role

How did you contribute to this situation? What's your part in the problem?

Step 3: Tell the Rest of the Story

What other explanations could there be? What might you be missing?

Step 4: Choose a Useful Story

What story would help you achieve your goals? What would help you take productive action?

Practice: Rewrite Your Stories

Common Scenario Examples:

Original Story: "My boss is micromanaging me because she doesn't trust me."

Alternative Story: "My boss is checking in frequently because she's under pressure from her own boss and wants to ensure we meet the deadline."

Original Story: "My teenager is being disrespectful and doesn't care about our family."

Alternative Story: "My teenager is trying to establish independence and is struggling with how to communicate their needs effectively."

The alternative stories don't excuse behavior, but they open up possibilities for dialogue and problem-solving.

The Emotional Freedom Formula

When you master your stories, you gain:

  • Emotional control - You choose your responses
  • Problem-solving power - You see multiple solutions
  • Relationship strength - You assume positive intent
  • Personal responsibility - You own your part
  • Dialogue readiness - You can approach others constructively

Remember:

Your emotions are your choice. By mastering your stories, you master your emotional responses and open the door to crucial conversations.
