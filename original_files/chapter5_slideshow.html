Chapter 5

Master My Stories

How to stay in dialogue when you're angry, scared, or hurt

Core Message

Others don't make you mad. You make you mad.

Between what we observe and how we feel, we tell ourselves a story. Change the story, change the emotion.

The Path to Action

SEE & HEAR
↓
TELL A STORY
↓
FEEL
↓
ACT

The story is where we have leverage and control

Discussion Question

Who makes us angry?

Take 3-4 minutes to discuss this with the group

Dan's Digest

Key Answer Points:

We make ourselves angry. While it's comfortable to say "he made me mad," others don't create our emotions—we do. Our emotions come from the stories we tell ourselves about what we observe, not from the observations themselves.

Worth Considering:

  • Why do different people have different emotional responses to the same situation?
  • The intermediate step between observation and emotion is the story we tell
  • Taking ownership of our emotions puts us back in control

Stories Create Feelings

What happens between seeing/hearing and feeling?

  • We observe what others do (facts)
  • We add meaning to the action (interpretation)
  • We make a guess at the motive (attribution)
  • We add judgment (good or bad)
  • Based on our story, our body responds with emotion
We tell ourselves a story so quickly that we don't even know we're doing it

Discussion Question

What role do stories play in the creation of our feelings and actions?

Take 4-5 minutes to discuss this with the group

Dan's Digest

Key Answer Points:

Stories are the link between what we observe and how we feel and act. They provide rationale for what's happening—our interpretations of facts. Once told, stories control us: they control how we feel, then how we act, and thus the results we get.

Worth Considering:

  • Any set of facts can be used to tell an infinite number of stories
  • If we take control of our stories, they won't control us
  • Maria's story about Louis: same facts, different stories create different emotions

Three Clever Stories

Self-justifying stories that allow us to feel good about behaving badly

Victim Stories

"It's not my fault"

Make us out to be innocent sufferers

Villain Stories

"It's all your fault"

Turn others into bad guys

Helpless Stories

"There's nothing I can do"

Make us powerless to act

Victim Stories

"It's not my fault"

  • Make us out to be innocent sufferers
  • We ignore the role we played in the problem
  • We speak only of our noble motives
  • We're being punished for our virtues, not our vices
Example: "I took longer because I was trying to beat the standard specs. He just doesn't appreciate a person with my superb attention to detail."

Villain Stories

"It's all your fault"

  • Turn normal, decent people into villains
  • Impute bad motives
  • Exaggerate their flaws into indictments
  • Ignore any of their virtues
  • Replace their name with a label ("bonehead," "control freak")
The Double Standard: When we make mistakes, our intentions were innocent. When others hurt us, they had terrible motives.

Helpless Stories

"There's nothing I can do"

  • Make ourselves powerless to do anything helpful
  • Convince ourselves there are no healthy alternatives
  • Justify the actions we're about to take
  • Often stem from villain stories
  • Offer fool's choices: "I can be honest and ruin the relationship, or stay silent and suffer"
Example: "If I didn't yell at my son, he wouldn't listen" or "If I told the boss, he'd just be defensive, so I say nothing"

Discussion Question

What are Victim, Villain, and Helpless Stories?

Take 4-5 minutes to discuss examples from your own experience

Dan's Digest

Key Answer Points:

These are self-justifying stories that allow us to feel good about behaving badly. Victim stories make us innocent sufferers. Villain stories turn others into bad guys. Helpless stories make us powerless to act. They're "clever" because they justify our ineffective behavior.

Worth Considering:

  • Which clever story do you tell most often?
  • These stories usually start after we've done something we feel we need to justify
  • They keep us stuck in ineffective behavior patterns

Separating Fact from Story

Spot the story by watching for hot words

Hot Words = Story (Judgments & Attributions)

"She scowled at me." • "He was sarcastic." • "He's controlling."

These are judgments/attributions that generate strong emotions—they're story, not fact.

Facts = Observable Behaviors (See/Hear)

"Her eyes pinched shut and her lips tightened."

"He spoke for 10 minutes and met with the boss 1:1."

Key tip: Ask, "Can I see or hear the thing I'm calling a 'fact'?"

From Story to Facts

Iterate until you're back to observable data

  • First attempt (all story): "Louis violated our plan, stole my slides, and forced me to the sidelines."
  • Second attempt (some facts): "Louis took 10 slides I was supposed to cover and never looked at me for questions."
  • Third attempt (more facts): "Louis covered 10 slides we had agreed I would cover; when questions were asked, he answered them."
  • Fourth attempt (even more facts + my role): "Louis covered 10 slides we'd agreed I would cover, and I let him. When questions came, he answered; I did not step in."
Why this matters: Each pass strips out judgments and adds your role, which calms emotions and reopens dialogue.

Scan for Other Facts

Once we pick a story, we tend to see only confirming evidence and miss disconfirming data.

  • Ask: "What facts might contradict my story?"
  • e.g., "Louis praised my work last month" or "He collaborates well with a colleague I respect."
Goal: Build a complete picture before acting.

Discussion Question

When we're feeling upset, why is it important to get back to the facts?

Take 3-4 minutes to discuss this with the group

Dan's Digest

Key Answer Points:

Facts help us abandon our absolute certainty and open up to dialogue. Getting back to observable behaviors (what we can see or hear) separates our interpretations from reality. This creates space for alternative explanations and softens our emotions.

Worth Considering:

  • "Hot words" (e.g., scowl, sarcastic, controlling) are story, not fact
  • Ask: "Can I see or hear this thing I'm calling a fact?"
  • Facts are specific, objective, and verifiable by any observer

Sellouts Lead to Clever Stories

Why we tell clever stories

  • Sometimes our conclusions match reality — but that's the exception.
  • More often they're convenient justifications after a sellout (we didn't do what we felt was right).

When we don't admit our errors, we invent stories that make us right and others wrong.

Common sellouts

  • You believed you should help/apologize/speak up — and didn't.
  • You said yes when you should've said no.
  • You failed to meet a commitment and didn't inform others.

Sellouts → justification → victim/villain/helpless stories.

Discussion Question

Why should we ask what role we may be playing in the problem?

Take 4-5 minutes to discuss this with the group

Dan's Digest

Key Answer Points:

Asking about our role forces us to face up to the fact that we contributed to the problem. It jars us out of victim stories and into accountability. Most often, when we face persistent problems, our role is one of silent complicity—we've said nothing.

Worth Considering:

  • Ask: "What am I pretending not to notice about my role in the problem?"
  • This turns victims into actors—we move from passive to active
  • It helps us see how selective our perception has been

Tell the Rest of the Story

Transform clever stories into useful ones

Turn Victims into Actors

What am I pretending not to notice about my role?

Turn Villains into Humans

Why would a reasonable, rational person do this?

Turn Helpless into Able

What do I really want? What should I do now?

Discussion Question

Why should we start by assuming that others are reasonable and rational?

Take 3-4 minutes to discuss this with the group

Dan's Digest

Key Answer Points:

This question humanizes others and softens our emotions. It provides alternative explanations for behavior, helping us worry less about intent and more about impact. It's not about excusing bad behavior—it's about getting ourselves in a position to have meaningful dialogue.

Worth Considering:

  • It relaxes our absolute certainty enough to allow for dialogue
  • Empathy often replaces judgment when we search for plausible answers
  • The goal: deal with our own stories and emotions first

The Hostage Negotiator

Ron McMillan learned about humanizing others from a man in a very high-risk occupation

When we humanize others, we're not excusing bad behaviors—we're helping ourselves be in a position to have meaningful dialogue.

Maria's New Story

What am I pretending not to notice?

I chose not to interrupt when Louis started covering my slides, and I didn't step in during Q&A.

Why would a reasonable person do this?

He cares about quality and may have been nervous. He might not realize I'm equally committed.

What do I really want?

A respectful working relationship and clarity on presenting roles.

What should I do now?

Schedule a conversation to debrief the presentation and agree on who covers what next time.

Retrace Your Path

When you find yourself in silence or violence:

  • Notice your behavior: Am I in silence or violence?
  • Get in touch with your feelings: What emotions am I feeling?
  • Analyze your stories: What story is creating these emotions?
  • Get back to the facts: What have I seen or heard?
  • Watch for clever stories: Am I playing victim, villain, or helpless?
  • Tell the rest of the story: Use the three questions

This Week's Challenge

When you notice strong emotions:

  • Pause and separate fact from story
  • Identify which clever story you're telling
  • Ask the three questions to tell the rest of the story

Key Takeaway

We are not hostages to our emotions. We create them through the stories we tell.

Master your stories, and you'll master your emotions and your crucial conversations.
