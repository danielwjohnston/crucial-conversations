CHAPTER 12

Yeah, But

Advice for tough cases

The “Yeah, But” Trap

The moment you think “this situation is different” is the moment you need these skills most.

Every tough case follows the same principles—what changes is the courage required, not the toolkit.

Reframe

“Yeah, but” is not a reason to abandon the skills. It's a signal you're facing stakes high enough to demand them.

The Conversation You've Been Tolerating

Scenario

Someone on your team makes comments that bother you—not egregious enough for HR, but enough to erode your working relationship. You've tolerated it for months. Now you're angry.

The Danger

The longer you wait, the bigger the Villain Story grows. By the time you speak up, your body language says what your words try to hide.

The Leadership Move

Own the delay. Use Contrasting: “I don't want to make this a bigger deal than it is—I do want to clear the air so we can work well together.” STATE your facts without the accumulated resentment.

The Person Who Does Only What They're Told

Scenario

Your direct report completes assigned tasks but never takes ownership. When they hit a problem, they try once and stop. You've been compensating—assigning backups, following up yourself.

The Leadership Trap

You've been acting out your low expectations instead of talking them out. Every workaround you create reinforces the pattern.

The Leadership Move

Address the pattern, not the instance. Be specific: “When you hit the roadblock last week, you left a voicemail and stopped. What else could you have tried?” Then jointly define what initiative looks like going forward.

Discussion Break #1

Scenario: A team member consistently does good work but never goes beyond what's explicitly asked. You've started assigning their most important tasks to someone else “just in case.” They haven't noticed—or haven't said anything.

Should you address the initiative gap, or the fact that you've been working around them? Which is the real conversation—Content, Pattern, or Relationship? What's the cost if you keep compensating instead of talking?

When Trust Breaks

Trust isn't binary—it comes in degrees and is topic-specific.

It also has two dimensions: motive (do they care?) and ability (can they deliver?).

The Leadership Trap

When trust breaks in one area, we let it bleed into everything. We tell a Villain Story that makes them untrustworthy across the board—which makes them act even less trustworthy. A self-defeating cycle.

The Leadership Move

Address trust around the specific issue, not the whole person. STATE what you see: “I need to hear the risks, not just the upside. Can we talk about what could go wrong?” Rebuild trust in small, specific increments.

The Deeply Personal Issue

Scenario

Someone has a hygiene problem, a social habit that's isolating them, or a personal issue affecting their work. Everyone talks about it—except to the person who needs to hear it.

The Hard Truth

Silence isn't kindness. When you withhold feedback to protect someone's feelings, you rob them of information they need. The longer you wait, the worse the eventual conversation.

The Leadership Move

Use Contrasting to separate intent from content: “I don't want to embarrass you—I do want to share something I think will help.” Be direct, be brief, then move to solutions. Don't pile on.

Discussion Break #2

Tradeoff A: A colleague has a personal issue (hygiene, social habit, etc.) that's affecting how others treat them. You care about this person. Is it more respectful to protect their feelings by staying silent—or to risk the awkwardness of telling them the truth?

Tradeoff B: You've lost trust in a team member after they missed a critical deadline. They're asking for a high-visibility assignment. How do you balance giving them a chance to rebuild trust vs. protecting the team from risk?

The Universal Truth

Every “yeah, but” scenario shares the same core challenge:

The conversation feels too risky, so we stay silent—and the problem compounds.

Three things that never change

  • Start with Heart: What do I really want—not what do I want to avoid?
  • Master My Stories: Separate facts from story before you open your mouth
  • Make It Safe: Make it safe enough for both of you to be honest

The question isn't “Is this situation different?” The question is “Am I willing to try?”

Discussion Break #3

Think about the conversation you've been avoiding—the one that came to mind during this session. Without naming names:

What's the cost of continuing to avoid it? Which skill from this book would help you most in that specific situation? What's one small step you could take this week to move toward it?

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #1)

The real conversation is almost always Pattern or Relationship. The initiative gap is a pattern. But the fact that you've been secretly working around them? That's a relationship issue—you've already lost confidence in them and are acting on it without telling them.

The trap of compensating: Every time you assign a backup or follow up yourself, you reinforce the person's current behavior. They have no reason to change because they don't know there's a problem. You've been acting out your expectations instead of talking them out.

The cost of silence: You eventually resent them, they sense something is off, and trust erodes—all without a single honest conversation.

Key Points

  • Use CPR to identify the real issue—most people stay at Content when they should escalate
  • Workarounds feel safer than conversations, but they make the problem worse
  • The person deserves to know your expectations have changed

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #2)

Tradeoff A — Silence vs. truth: The book is clear: silence isn't kindness. But the how matters enormously. Contrasting separates your good intent from the painful content. The goal is to share information the person needs—briefly, respectfully, privately—and then move to solutions, not dwell on the problem.

Tradeoff B — Trust vs. risk: Trust is rebuilt in small, specific increments. You don't have to say yes or no—you can say “yes, with checkpoints.” STATE what you need: “I want to give you this opportunity. I also need to know early if things go off track. Can we agree on weekly check-ins?” That's not micromanaging—it's rebuilding trust transparently.

Key Points

  • Trust has two dimensions: motive and ability. Diagnose which one broke.
  • Don't let broken trust in one area become a Villain Story about the whole person
  • The leadership move is always: talk it out, don't act it out

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #3)

The cost question is the key. Most people can articulate the cost of the avoided conversation when asked directly. The cost is usually compounding: resentment builds, trust erodes, workarounds accumulate, and the eventual conversation gets harder every week you delay.

One small step: You don't have to have the full conversation this week. You could write out your STATE opening. You could prepare a contrasting statement. You could simply decide on the right topic using CPR. Preparation is action.

Key Points

  • “Yeah, but” is a signal, not an excuse
  • The conversation you're avoiding is usually the one that matters most
  • Courage + skill > courage alone. Prepare before you leap.
