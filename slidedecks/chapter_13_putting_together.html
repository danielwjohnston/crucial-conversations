CHAPTER 13

Putting It All Together

Tools for preparing and learning

The Two Principles That Matter Most

After everything we've covered, the authors say two things matter above all else:

Learn to Look

The single most important thing you can do is notice when a conversation turns crucial—and notice when you or others leave dialogue. Even if you can't remember a single acronym, asking “Are we in dialogue right now?” changes everything.

Make It Safe

When you notice dialogue has broken down, your only job is to make it safer. Apologize, contrast, ask a question, take a timeout—anything. 9 times out of 10, simply realizing that safety is the problem will help you find a solution.

Why These Two? The Concept of Lag Time

The problem isn't that we have problems. The problem is lag time.

Lag time is the gap between when we know there's an issue and when we actually address it. Reduce the lag, and everything gets better.

Learn to Look

Reduces lag time by helping you catch problems early—before they compound.

Make It Safe

Reduces lag time by giving you the confidence to speak up instead of waiting.

Everything else in the book—STATE, AMPP, CURE, WWWF—serves these two principles. They're the tools. Learn to Look and Make It Safe are the mindset.

When You're Stuck: Diagnose & Fix

Start with Heart: What do I really want?

Master My Stories: Separate fact from story

The other person shuts down or blows up

Make It Safe: Step out of content, rebuild safety

Case Study: The Estate Conversation

The Situation

Two siblings must divide their mother's estate. One provided years of daily caregiving; the other contributed financially from a distance. Both feel unappreciated. Both have Villain Stories about the other.

What Makes It Work

  • The caregiving sibling uses Master My Stories before the conversation—recognizing they never asked for help and now blame their sister for not offering
  • They use Contrasting to separate the financial discussion from the emotional one
  • They use AMPP to draw out their sister's perspective instead of assuming the worst

The breakthrough comes not from a perfect script, but from the willingness to stay in dialogue when every instinct says to fight or flee.

The Preparation Tool

Before any crucial conversation, write down:

1. What do I really want? (for me, for them, for the relationship)

2. What are the facts? (what I've actually seen or heard—no interpretation)

3. What's my story? (the meaning I've added to those facts)

4. How will I make it safe? (my contrasting statement, my statement of good intent)

5. How will I move to action? (WWWF)

Five minutes of preparation dramatically changes the outcome. Paper beats your head.

The Real Lesson

This book isn't about communication. It's about results.

Every important result in your life—at work, at home, in your community—lives on the other side of a conversation you're either having well or avoiding entirely.

The Authors' Closing Argument

  • You don't have to be perfect. You just have to try.
  • Your everyday conversations are your greatest teachers. Watch and listen for crucial conversations happening all around you.
  • When you're unsure what to do, fall back to the basics: get to dialogue and stay there.

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #1)

Common “aha” moments: Many people are surprised by Master My Stories—realizing they make themselves angry with stories, not facts. Others find Make It Safe transformative: safety is about intent, not content.

Why the hardest skill is hard: It usually goes against your natural tendencies. Silent types struggle with STATE—speaking up confidently. Forceful types struggle with listening (AMPP). The emotional state you're in matters as much as the skill itself—that's why Master My Stories comes before STATE in the model.

Key Points

  • The complete model is a toolkit, not a script—use what you need in the moment
  • Start with the skill that addresses your biggest gap
  • Awareness is the first step—you can't fix what you don't see

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #2)

Preparation is power: The five-step prep tool (Want, Facts, Story, Safety, Action) takes 5 minutes and dramatically improves outcomes. Writing it down exposes gaps in your thinking that stay hidden when you prepare in your head.

Start small: Don't pick your hardest conversation first. Build confidence with medium-stakes situations, then work up. The goal is to build a habit of preparation, not to tackle the biggest issue immediately.

Debrief is essential: Every conversation is practice. After the conversation, ask: What went well? What would I do differently? Skill comes from reflection, not just repetition.

Key Points

  • Write it down—preparation on paper beats preparation in your head
  • The most revealing question is often #3: “What's my story?”
  • One skill at a time for 30 days builds lasting habits

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Final Reflection)

The “handle differently” question: Most people can immediately identify a conversation from the past year that they'd approach differently. The most common change? They'd have it sooner—reducing lag time is the single biggest improvement people report.

The 30-day commitment: Pick the skill that addresses your biggest gap, not the one that feels easiest. Common choices: Master My Stories (for people who get emotional), Make It Safe (for people who avoid), STATE (for people who hint instead of being direct).

The advice question: This question reveals what resonated most deeply. There's no wrong answer—but it's a powerful way to crystallize your own takeaway from the bookclub.

Key Points

  • You're building new neural pathways—be patient with yourself
  • The goal is progress, not perfection
  • Your challenge is not to be perfect. Your challenge is to be in dialogue.
