Retake Your Pen

How to Be Resilient When Hearing Tough Feedback

"No one can hurt me without my permission."
— Mahatma Gandhi

The Wedding Toast Disaster

One of the authors once gave a lengthy wedding toast to a happy couple. Every time he referred to the bride, he erroneously used the name of the groom's ex-wife.

He was feeling flush with confidence in his artful delivery, until after his fourth use of the wrong name...

The Moment:

At that point, a cringing guest couldn't take it anymore and yelled out, "It's Bonnie, not Becky!"

Oops.

Think About It:

How would you feel if someone gave you feedback like that in front of everyone?

Getting Feedback vs. Being Feed-Smacked

Critical feedback can be hard to hear. Some of the most crucial conversations of all are when others tell you unpleasant things about yourself.

But there's a difference between getting feedback and being feed-smacked.

Most of us have been feed-smacked at some point in our lives. In the middle of a meeting, a colleague says something that feels like a personal attack. Or during a performance review, your boss delivers criticism that feels unfair and unhelpful.

The Question:

How do you receive feedback without becoming defensive or shutting down?

The Challenge: Receiving Feedback

When we receive feedback, our natural tendency is to:

Common Reactions:

  • Defend ourselves - "That's not fair!"
  • Explain our intentions - "I didn't mean to..."
  • Blame others - "But you were the one who..."
  • Shut down - Silence, withdrawal
  • Attack back - "Well, you're not perfect either!"

The Problem:

These reactions prevent us from learning and growing. They damage relationships and shut down dialogue.

How to Retake Your Pen

The key to receiving feedback well is to "retake your pen" - to take control of your emotional response and choose how to react.

Step 1: Breathe

Take a moment to calm your initial emotional reaction.

Step 2: Look for the Truth

Ask yourself: Is there any truth in this feedback? Even 1%?

Step 3: Clarify Understanding

Ask questions to make sure you understand what they mean.

Step 4: Thank Them

Appreciate their courage and willingness to share feedback.

Look for the Truth

Even poorly delivered feedback often contains valuable information.

Ask Yourself:

  • What part of this feedback is accurate?
  • What could I learn from this?
  • How might this feedback help me grow?
  • What patterns am I not seeing?
  • What would someone I respect say about this?

The Goal:

Find the kernel of truth, even if it's wrapped in poor delivery.

Focus on the message, not the delivery.

Clarify and Thank

Clarifying Questions:

  • "Can you give me an example of when I did that?"
  • "What would you suggest I do differently?"
  • "How does this affect you/the team/the project?"
  • "What's the impact when I do X?"

Gratitude Statements:

  • "Thank you for sharing that with me."
  • "I appreciate you being honest with me."
  • "That's helpful feedback, thank you."
  • "I know that wasn't easy to say, so thank you."

Remember:

People who give feedback are taking a risk. Acknowledge their courage.
