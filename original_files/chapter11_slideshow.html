The Finish Line Problem

Why We Fail to Convert Ideas into Action

Problem #1: Unclear Expectations

We have unclear expectations about how decisions will be made.

People may not understand who decides, or how their input will be used.

Problem #2: Poor Follow-Through

We do a poor job of acting on the decisions we do make.

Ideas slip away, dissipate, or people can't figure out what to do with them.

Feeling better is not the same as getting better.

Dialog Is Not Decision-Making

The Two Riskiest Times in Crucial Conversations

The Beginning â€” You have to find a way to create safety, or else things go awry.

The End â€” If you aren't careful about how you clarify conclusions and decisions, you can run into violated expectations later on.

This happens in two ways: people don't understand how decisions will be made, or no decision gets made at all.

When Agreement Isn't Agreement

Cara and Rene's Cruise

A week ago, Cara and Rene had a Crucial Conversation about vacation plans. Both expressed their views respectfully and candidly. At the end, they decided a cruise suited both quite well.

Then Rene forwarded a confirmation emailâ€”a 3-day cruise, $500 deposit for an outside suite.

Cara is upset. Rene is bewilderedâ€”he thought Cara would be ecstatic.

What happened? Cara agreed in principle about a cruise. She didn't agree to this particular cruise. Rene thought any cruise would be fine and made a decision on his own.

Have fun on that cruise, Rene.

When No Decision Gets Made

Crucial Conversations are tough. By the time we navigate successfully to the end, we're often so incredibly relieved that we simply end with:

"Thanks. I'm really glad we could have this conversation."

We walk away feeling better because no one cried, no one yelled. Chalk one up in the win column.

But because we haven't clarified our understanding or solidified decisions, ideas slip away and dissipate, or people can't figure out what to do with them.

Decide How to Decide

Dialog is a process for getting all relevant meaning into a shared pool. That process involves everyone.

But allowing people to share their meaning doesn't mean they are guaranteed to take part in making all the decisions.

To avoid violated expectations: Separate dialog from decision-making.

Make it clear how decisions will be madeâ€”who will be involved and why.

Who Decides How to Decide?

When Authority Is Clear

When you're in a position of authority, you decide which method to use.

VPs don't ask hourly employees to decide on pricing. Parents don't ask toddlers to set bedtimes.

Deciding what decisions to turn over and when is part of leadership stewardship.

When Authority Is Unclear

When there's no clear line of authority, deciding how to decide is difficult.

Example: Teacher suggests holding your child back. Whose choice is it? Who decides whose choice it is?

Put "how we'll decide" into the pool of shared meaning.

When Authority Is Unclear: What to Do

The School Decision

Your daughter's teacher suggests holding her back a grade. You're not sure. But whose choice is this anyway?

  • Does everyone have a say, then a vote?
  • Is it the school officials' responsibility?
  • Should parents consult with experts and then decide?

If you don't openly talk about who decides and why, you're likely to end up in a heated battleâ€”possibly in court: The Jones Family vs. Happy Valley School District.

The solution: Talk openly about your child's abilities and about how the final choice will be made. Don't mention lawyers in your opening comments. Use your best dialog skills to jointly decide how to decide.

Four Methods of Decision-Making

These represent increasing degrees of involvement. More involvement = more commitment, but less efficiency.

1. Command

Decisions made with no involvement. Either within your authority, external forces dictate, or you delegate completely.

2. Consult

Decision makers invite others to influence them before making the choice. Gather ideas, evaluate, decide, inform.

3. Vote

Best when efficiency is highest value and selecting from good options. Only use when everyone will support the outcome.

4. Consensus

Talk until everyone honestly agrees. High commitment, high cost. Reserve for high-stakes decisions.

Four Questions for Choosing the Method

1. Who Cares?

Determine who genuinely wants to be involved, along with those affected.

Don't involve people who don't care.

2. Who Knows?

Identify who has the expertise you need for the best decision.

Try not to involve people who contribute no new information.

3. Who Must Agree?

Think of those whose cooperation you need in the form of authority or influence.

Better to involve them than to surprise them.

4. How Many People?

Involve the fewest number while considering quality and support.

Ask: Do we have enough? Will others need to commit?

Say It Out Loud

The Product Meeting Disaster

You send a meeting invite to "discuss new product features." The discussion is robust. You end with pretty clear consensus among experts.

Then you review market research, get finance feedback, do customer testing. You take all this in and make your decision.

You email the group. Within minutes, your inbox floods:

"Why did you even bother to involve us if you were just going to do whatever you wanted anyway?"

What happened? You knew it was a consult decision. They assumed consensus.

How to Communicate the Method

Once you've decided how you'll decide, make sure everyone knows. It can be as simple as:

FOR A CONSULT DECISION:

"Your input is critical here. And please be aware, this is a consult decision. I'll take your input along with that of others and make the decision."

FOR TIME-BOUNDED CONSENSUS:

"I'd like for this to be a consensus decision. But we need to decide today, and we only have an hour. If we can come to consensus in that time frame, great. If not, I'll take all your input and make the final decision."

This seems obvious. We marvel at how often it's overlooked.

Make Assignments: Put Decisions into Action

Does every conversation need to end with a decision? Not necessarily.

But while a conversation doesn't always need to end with a decision, it should always end with a commitmentâ€”even if it's just a commitment to reflect on what was shared.

The WWWF Framework

Who does What by When?

How will you Follow up?

W

Who?

"Everybody's business is nobody's business."

â€” English Proverb

If you don't make an actual assignment to an actual person, there's a good chance nothing will ever come of all the work you've gone through.

There is no "we" when it comes to assignments.

"We" actually means "not me." It's code. Even when people aren't trying to duck, the term "we" leads them to believe others are taking responsibility.

Assign a name to every responsibility. If multiple people are involved, appoint one as the responsible party.

W

Does What?

Be sure to spell out the exact deliverables. The fuzzier the expectations, the higher the likelihood of disappointment.

Howard Hughes' Steam Car

Hughes assigned engineers to design the world's first steam-powered car. He gave them virtually no direction.

After years of work, they produced a prototype by running dozens of pipes through the car's bodyâ€”essentially a giant radiator.

When Hughes asked what would happen in a wreck, they nervously explained: "Passengers would be boiled alive, much like lobsters in a pot."

Hughes was so upset he had them cut it into pieces no larger than three inches. End of project.

Learn from Hughes

Clarify boundary conditions up front:

"I want a steam-powered car that is at least as safe, cost-effective, and feature-rich as petroleum-powered cars."

Use Contrasting

If you've seen people misunderstand in the past, explain the common mistake as what you don't want.

Point to Examples

Rather than talk abstractly, bring a prototype or sample. Point to pictures of what you want and don't want.

The Set Designer Lesson

A renowned designer promised "a modern open office that transforms to a manufacturing look." Tens of thousands of dollars later: Star Trek, not Silicon Valley. We started over from scratch.

Now we say: "Don't use furniture unfamiliar to Fortune 500 employees. DO make it resemble these pictures."

W

By When?

It's shocking how often people leave this element out.

The Problem with "Someday"

Instead of giving a deadline, people point to the setting sun of "someday."

With vague or unspoken deadlines:

  • Other urgencies come up
  • The assignment finds its way to the bottom of the pile
  • It is soon forgotten

Assignments without deadlines are far better at producing guilt than stimulating action.

Goals without deadlines aren't goalsâ€”they're merely directions.

F

How Will You Follow Up?

Always agree on how often and by what method you'll follow up.

SIMPLE CONFIRMATION:

"Call me on my cell when you finish your homework. Then you can go play with friends. OK?"

MILESTONES:

"Let me know when you've completed your library research. Then we'll sit down and look at the next steps."

MILESTONES + DROP-DEAD DATE:

"Let me know as soon as you've completed the research component. You've got until the last week in November, but if you finish earlier, give me a call."

If you want people to feel accountable, you must give them an opportunity to account.

WWWF for Personal Conversations

How do you apply this without sounding bureaucratic? Three tips:

1. SUMMARIZE FOR UNDERSTANDING:

"Great. This has been a really helpful conversation. I want to recap what we've talked about just to make sure I have it all right."

2. IDENTIFY AN ACTION:

"I'm so glad we've had this conversation. I want to make sure I'm clear on what we each need to do differently going forward. In terms of my commitments, I'll..."

3. PLAN A CHECK-IN (not check-up):

"I'll circle back next week for a quick check, just to make sure that after we've had some time to sit with this, everything still seems OK and on track for both of us."

Document Your Work

"One dull pencil is worth six sharp minds."

Don't leave your hard work to memory. If you've gone to the effort to complete a Crucial Conversation, don't fritter away all the meaning you created by trusting your memories.

  • Write down conclusions, decisions, and assignments
  • Record who does what by when
  • Revisit your notes at key times (usually the next meeting)
  • Hold people accountable using the STATE skills

When you hold people accountable, you don't just increase their motivationâ€”you create a culture of integrity.
