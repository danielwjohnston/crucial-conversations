CHAPTER 5

Master My Stories

How to stay in dialogue when you're angry, scared, or hurt

Core Message

Others don't make you mad. You make you mad.

Between what we observe and how we feel, we tell ourselves a story. Change the story, change the emotion.

The Path to Action

SEE & HEAR
↓
TELL A STORY
↓
FEEL
↓
ACT

The story is where we have leverage and control

Stories Create Feelings

What happens between seeing/hearing and feeling?

  • We observe what others do (facts)
  • We add meaning to the action (interpretation)
  • We make a guess at the motive (attribution)
  • We add judgment (good or bad)
  • Based on our story, our body responds with emotion
We tell ourselves a story so quickly that we don't even know we're doing it

Three Clever Stories

Self-justifying stories that allow us to feel good about behaving badly

Victim Stories

"It's not my fault"

Make us out to be innocent sufferers. We ignore the role we played.

Villain Stories

"It's all your fault"

Turn normal people into villains. We impute bad motives and exaggerate flaws.

Helpless Stories

"There's nothing I can do"

Make us powerless to act. We convince ourselves there are no healthy alternatives.

The Double Standard: When we make mistakes, our intentions were innocent. When others hurt us, they had terrible motives.

Discussion Break #1

The book says "others don't make you mad—you make you mad." Think of a recent situation where you felt a strong negative emotion. What story were you telling yourself, and which clever story pattern (Victim, Villain, or Helpless) was driving it?

Separating Fact from Story

Spot the story by watching for "hot words"

Hot Words = Story (Judgments & Attributions)

"She scowled at me." • "He was sarcastic." • "He's controlling."

These are judgments/attributions that generate strong emotions—they're story, not fact.

Facts = Observable Behaviors (See/Hear)

"Her eyes pinched shut and her lips tightened."

"He spoke for 10 minutes and met with the boss 1:1."

Key tip: Ask, "Can I see or hear the thing I'm calling a 'fact'?"

From Story to Facts

Iterate until you're back to observable data

  • First attempt (all story): "Louis violated our plan, stole my slides, and forced me to the sidelines."
  • Second attempt (some facts): "Louis took 10 slides I was supposed to cover and never looked at me for questions."
  • Third attempt (more facts): "Louis covered 10 slides we had agreed I would cover; when questions were asked, he answered them."
  • Fourth attempt (facts + my role): "Louis covered 10 slides we'd agreed I would cover, and I let him. He answered questions; I did not step in."
Why this matters: Each pass strips out judgments and adds your role, which calms emotions and reopens dialogue.

Sellouts & Tell the Rest of the Story

When we don't admit our errors, we invent stories that make us right. A sellout is when we didn't do what we felt was right—then we justify it with a clever story.

Transform clever stories into useful ones:

Turn Victims into Actors

"What am I pretending not to notice about my role?"

Turn Villains into Humans

"Why would a reasonable, rational person do this?"

Turn Helpless into Able

"What do I really want? What should I do now?"

The Hostage Negotiator

Ron McMillan learned about humanizing others from a man in a very high-risk occupation

When we humanize others, we're not excusing bad behaviors—we're helping ourselves be in a position to have meaningful dialogue.

Maria's New Story

What am I pretending not to notice?

I chose not to interrupt when Louis started covering my slides, and I didn't step in during Q&A.

Why would a reasonable person do this?

He cares about quality and may have been nervous. He might not realize I'm equally committed.

What do I really want?

A respectful working relationship and clarity on presenting roles.

What should I do now?

Schedule a conversation to debrief the presentation and agree on who covers what next time.

Retrace Your Path

When you find yourself in silence or violence:

  • Notice your behavior: Am I in silence or violence?
  • Get in touch with your feelings: What emotions am I feeling?
  • Analyze your stories: What story is creating these emotions?
  • Get back to the facts: What have I seen or heard?
  • Watch for clever stories: Am I playing victim, villain, or helpless?
  • Tell the rest of the story: Use the three questions

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #1)

Who makes us angry? We make ourselves angry. Others don't create our emotions—we do. Our emotions come from the stories we tell ourselves about what we observe, not from the observations themselves.

The role of stories: Stories are the link between what we observe and how we feel and act. They provide rationale for what's happening. Once told, stories control us—how we feel, how we act, and the results we get.

Three Clever Stories: Self-justifying stories that allow us to feel good about behaving badly. Victim stories make us innocent sufferers. Villain stories turn others into bad guys. Helpless stories make us powerless to act.

Key Points

  • Why do different people have different emotional responses to the same situation? Because they tell different stories.
  • Any set of facts can be used to tell an infinite number of stories.
  • If we take control of our stories, they won't control us.

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #2)

Getting back to facts: Facts help us abandon absolute certainty and open up to dialogue. Getting back to observable behaviors separates our interpretations from reality, creating space for alternative explanations.

Humanizing others: Asking "Why would a reasonable person do this?" softens emotions and provides alternative explanations. It's not about excusing bad behavior—it's about getting ourselves in a position to have meaningful dialogue.

Our role in the problem: Asking about our role forces us to face up to our contribution. It jars us out of victim stories and into accountability. Most often, our role is silent complicity—we've said nothing.

Key Points

  • "Hot words" (scowl, sarcastic, controlling) are story, not fact—ask "Can I see or hear this?"
  • Empathy often replaces judgment when we search for plausible answers.
  • The goal: deal with our own stories and emotions first, then enter dialogue.
