CHAPTER 10

Retake Your Pen

How to be resilient and hear almost anything

The Pen: Your Power to Define Worth

Think of your pen as the power to define your worth.

When You Hold Your Pen

Your personal security comes from an innate sense of your enduring worth—not from others' opinions.

When You Lose Your Pen

Whoever holds your pen composes the terms of your well-being. You no longer enjoy praise—you need it.

“If you live by the compliment, you'll die by the criticism.”

We're born with our pen firmly in our grasp. But as we grow, we hand it to others without realizing it.

Lessons from The Other Side Academy

In Salt Lake City, 120 people arrested an average of 25 times each run businesses together to overcome lifetimes of self-destruction.

Tuesday and Friday Nights: Games

Students give each other unvarnished feedback for 15-20 minutes. Vocabulary is raw. The fundamental belief:

“Relentless exposure to truth is the best path to empathy, growth, and happiness.”

Older students advise: “Just listen, put it in a bag, take it to your pillow. There you decide what's gold and what's crap.”

Marlin Takes His Game

A younger student brings Marlin a full-throated game: “You're a control freak! It has to be your way! Why can't you let me do it my way sometimes?”

Marlin sits calmly, face becoming sad. Then: “I didn't think about how this felt to you. You're right. That is what I do. I'll fix it.”

Three years ago, Marlin feared truth and craved approval. Today, he craves truth and fears approval.

Discussion Break #1

Think of a time feedback was particularly hard to hear. Looking back, who was holding your “pen”—your sense of worth—in that moment? What would have changed if you'd held onto it?

Two Parts of the Pen

1. Safety

We equate approval with safety and disapproval with danger, failing to update our capacity to protect ourselves.

“You don't get angry when you're confident. You get angry when you're scared.”

2. Worth

We live worrying we aren't worthy. Others' feedback becomes an audit of our worth.

When we surrender our pen, we stop generating feelings of worth and start looking for them.

Two assumptions: Truth is an absolute good. Feedback is usually a mixture—sort what's true and discard the rest.

The CURE Framework

Collect Yourself

Breathe deeply. Name your feelings. Connect with soothing truths: “This can't hurt me. I'm safe.”

Understand

Be curious. Ask questions and examples. Focus on understanding, not defending.

Recover

Ask for a timeout. Feelings of control bring feelings of safety. “I need time, and I'll get back to you.”

Engage

Look for truth, not holes. Even if 95% junk and 5% gold—find the gold. Then commit to what you'll do.

Discussion Break #2

Which part of the CURE framework would be most helpful for you to practice? How can you prepare yourself in advance to hear tough feedback without losing your pen?

This Week's Challenge

Practice retaking your pen:

  • Write a personal affirmation about your worth (like Marlin's)
  • Next time you receive feedback, pause and use CURE
  • Separate the gold from the crap—even harsh feedback usually has a kernel of truth

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #1)

The pen metaphor: Your pen is the power to define your worth. When you hold it, others' opinions are information. When you've handed it away, any feedback can feel devastating.

Why feedback hurts: The pain doesn't track with content or delivery. It tracks with who holds your pen. Carmen's uncle's comment stuck for decades because she'd given him authority over her worth.

We're born holding our pen. Babies don't fret over others' opinions. We hand it away gradually as we seek external validation.

Key Points

  • If you live by the compliment, you'll die by the criticism
  • The biggest variable isn't the message—it's who held your pen in that moment
  • Personal security comes from within, not from others' approval

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #2)

CURE is sequential: You can't Understand if you haven't Collected yourself first. Most people struggle with Collect (staying calm) or Engage (looking for truth).

Prepare in advance: Practice affirming truths before you need them. Build your foundation in low-stakes situations first.

Feedback is a mixture: Your job isn't to swallow it whole—it's to sort what's true and discard the rest.

Key Points

  • Collect is foundational—without it, the other steps are nearly impossible
  • TOSA students say: “I will take a look at that”—promising to look sincerely on their own timeline
  • Even if 95% junk, find the 5% gold
