Chapter 9

Explore Others' Paths

How to listen when others blow up or clam up

The Scenario: Sanj's Dilemma

The Project Meeting

Sanj asks his team for risks on a critical project plan. He gets silence. Blank stares. No one speaks up.

Later, he asks Tony privately. Tony explodes into violence:

"Everyone knows this project is a train ride to disaster land... You're that guy from corporate on his white horse... I'm not taking the fall when this goes south!"

When others move to silence or violence, we often react by joining them.

The Goal: Restore Safety

Explore Others' Paths

To get to the truth, you have to make it safe for others to share their truth.

When others blow up or clam up, the best way to bring them back to dialogue is to let them know it's okay to share their path to action—even their nasty stories and ugly feelings.

Start with Heart

Be Curious, Not Furious

When people become furious, we need to become curious.

The Bad Question

When we feel attacked, we naturally ask: "What is wrong with this person?" (Assigning bad motives)

The Good Question

Distract your brain from judgment by asking: "Why would a reasonable, rational, and decent person say this?"

Retracing the Path

We join the conversation at the end

We usually see their Action (yelling or silence) first. We need to help them trace it back to the source.

Action (Silence/Violence) ← Emotion ← Story ← Facts

Every sentence has a history. If you don't get to the facts, you're just arguing about the emotions.

Power Listening Tools (AMPP)

Use these skills to encourage others to share their path

A

Ask

Start by simply expressing interest in their views.

M

Mirror

Respectfully acknowledge the emotions they appear to be feeling.

P

Paraphrase

Restate what you've heard to show you understand.

P

Prime

Take your best guess at what they are thinking/feeling.

Ask

Get things rolling

The easiest way to encourage someone to share is to invite them.

Common Invitations

  • "What's going on?"
  • "I'd really like to hear your opinion on this."
  • "Please let me know if you see it differently."
  • "Don't worry about hurting my feelings. I really want to hear your thoughts."

Mirror

Confirm feelings when words and tone don't match

Use this when their tone of voice or gestures hints at emotions that contradict their words.

Person: "Don't worry, I'm fine." (Said while looking away and kicking the ground)

You (Mirroring): "Really? From the way you're saying that, it doesn't sound like you are. You seem upset."

Important: Keep your tone calm. You are describing what you see, not mocking them.

Paraphrase

Acknowledge the story

When you get a clue about why they are feeling that way, restate it in your own words.

Example

You: "Let's see if I've got this right. You're worried because the previous project manager was fired, and you're wondering if the team might be at risk too. Is that it?"

Don't just parrot back. Put the message in abbreviated form to show you are trying to understand.

Prime

When you're getting nowhere

Sometimes people feel unsafe and won't talk even after you Ask, Mirror, and Paraphrase. You may need to "Prime the Pump."

How to Prime

Take your best guess at what they might be thinking and say it out loud (tentatively) to make it safe to discuss.

"Are you thinking that the only reason we're doing this is to make money? That maybe we don't care about your personal lives?"

Discussion Question

Think of a recent situation where someone "clammed up" (Silence) or "blew up" (Violence). Which of the AMPP skills (Ask, Mirror, Paraphrase, Prime) would have been most useful to get them back to dialogue?

Take 5 minutes to discuss.

What If You Disagree?

Remember your ABCs

Once they have shared their path, it is your turn. But what if their facts are wrong? What if you completely disagree?

Don't move to argument. Move to ABC.

  • Agree
  • Build
  • Compare

Agree

Don't turn an agreement into an argument

Violent Agreement: When two people actually agree on the main point but fight over the 5-10% of details they differ on.

If you agree with the other person's path, say so and move on. Start with an area of agreement.

"I agree with you that we need to be careful about the timeline..."

Build

When they leave something out

We are trained to look for errors (the "Teacher's Pet" syndrome). Instead of saying "Wrong!", try building.

The Method

If you agree with what was said but the info is incomplete:

1. Point out the agreement.

2. Add elements that were left out.

"Absolutely. In addition, I noticed that..."

Compare

When you differ significantly

If you truly disagree, don't suggest they are "wrong." Suggest that you differ.

Don't Say

"No, that's wrong. Here is the truth..."

Do Say

"I see things differently. Let me describe how I come at this..."

Use your STATE skills (Chapter 8) to share your path, then invite them to compare the two views.

Discussion Question

Do you have a tendency to fall into "Violent Agreement"? Do you find yourself nitpicking minor differences (Build) or flat out saying "You're wrong" (Compare)?

Discuss how using "Yes, and..." (Build) or "I see it differently" (Compare) changes the tone.

Sanj's Turnaround

Putting it all together

Sanj returns to the team meeting where Andrea is defensive about the deadline.

Sanj (Mirror): "The way you say that... makes me wonder if you trust me."

Sanj (Prime): "I've heard references to 'Singapore Sanj'... Are you worried that because I'm from corporate, I'm just here to evaluate you?"

Andrea: "Well, Sanj... yes. If that is a concern, I want to address it..."

By exploring their path (even the nasty parts), Sanj got to the real issue: Trust and Job Security, not just timelines.

Success Story: Darrell Kay

From Deal-Breaker to Deal-Maker

Darrell was negotiating a venture capital deal that was falling apart. He was being bullied on the phone at 5 AM.

The Change: "I had to fight my impulse to argue for my view and instead restore safety by simply exploring the other side's perspective."

Result: A successful 17-page agreement that saved the partnership.

Set Expectations Up Front

The "Uncle Carl" Strategy

What if you explore their path, but they won't listen to yours?

You: "I really want to understand your point of view. I'm committing to listening with an open mind. I wonder if, once I've done that, you'd be willing to listen to my point of view?"

Most people will feel an obligation to reciprocate if you listen sincerely first.

Practice This Week

Pick a conversation where someone closes up:

  • Retrace their path: Go from their Action back to their Facts.
  • Use AMPP: Ask, Mirror, Paraphrase, or Prime to get them talking.
  • Use ABC: When it's your turn, Agree, Build, or Compare.

Key Takeaway

One of the best ways to persuade others is with your ears.

When you listen to others' deep concerns and "prime" the pump for their real stories, you build the safety needed to solve the real problems.
