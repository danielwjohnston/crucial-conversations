CHAPTER 11

Move to Action

How to turn crucial conversations into action and results

Core Message

Dialogue without decision is just talk

Two things commonly prevent people from converting dialogue into action and results:

  • No decision gets made. People walk away without agreeing on how to decide.
  • Violated expectations. People thought they agreed but had different interpretations.

Four Methods of Decision Making

Command

Decisions are made without involving others. Use when: stakes are low, it's not worth everyone's time, or someone has clear authority.

Consult

Input is gathered, then a subset decides. Use when: many are affected, expertise is needed, but not everyone must agree.

Vote

An agreed-upon percentage swings the decision. Use when: efficiency matters and the group can support any outcome.

Consensus

Everyone comes to agreement. Use when: high-stakes, everyone must genuinely support the decision.

How to Choose a Method

Ask four questions:

  • Who cares? Don't involve people who don't care.
  • Who knows? Include those with relevant expertise.
  • Who must agree? Include those whose cooperation you need.
  • How many people is it worth involving? More involvement = more commitment but more time.

Key principle: Whenever possible, make decisions by command or consult. Save consensus for high-stakes decisions.

Discussion Break #1

Think of a decision that caused frustration—at work or home. Was the frustration about the decision itself or about how it was made? Which of the four methods was actually used, and which should have been used?

WWWF: Make Assignments, Not Just Decisions

Who?

Assign a specific person. Avoid “we”—it means nobody.

Does What?

Spell out the exact deliverables. No vague verbs.

By When?

Set a specific deadline. “Sometime” = never.

How Will You Follow Up?

Always build in a check-in. No follow-up = no accountability.

Document it. Write down WWWF and track assignments. Don't rely on memory.

Discussion Break #2

Think of a conversation that ended with good intentions but nothing changed. What was missing from the WWWF framework—was it unclear ownership, vague deliverables, no deadline, or no follow-up?

This Week's Challenge

After your next crucial conversation:

  • Decide how to decide—don't leave the method ambiguous
  • Document WWWF for every action item
  • Follow up at the agreed-upon time

Next Meeting: Share how documenting WWWF changed outcomes

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #1)

Method matters: Most frustration comes from how decisions are made, not what's decided. When people feel excluded from a decision they care about, they resist the outcome.

Four questions: Who cares? Who knows? Who must agree? How many is it worth involving? These guide you to the right method.

Default to efficiency: Use command or consult when possible. Save consensus for high-stakes decisions where full buy-in is essential.

Key Points

  • Decide how to decide before deciding
  • More involvement = more commitment but more time
  • People support what they help create

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #2)

WWWF prevents drift: Without clear Who, What, When, and Follow-up, even the best conversations produce no results. “We should probably...” means nobody will.

Document everything: Write it down. Don't rely on memory. Track assignments visibly.

Follow up is accountability: Without a check-in, there's no mechanism to ensure things happen.

Key Points

  • Assign to a person, not “we” or “the team”
  • Specific deadlines, not “soon” or “when you get a chance”
  • No follow-up = no accountability
