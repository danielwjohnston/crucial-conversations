CHAPTER 7

Make It Safe

How to make it safe to talk about almost anything

Core Message

When safety is at risk, step out of the content and rebuild safety

People never become defensive about what you're saying—they become defensive because of why they think you're saying it.

Safety is about intent, not content.

Step Out, Make It Safe, Step Back In

1. Notice safety is at risk

Watch for silence or violence

2. Step out of the content

Stop talking about the topic

3. Rebuild safety

Use the four skills in this chapter

4. Step back in

Return to the conversation once safety is restored

Two Conditions of Safety

For people to feel safe, they need to know two things about your intent:

Mutual Purpose

The Entrance Condition

Others perceive you're working toward a common outcome—you care about their goals, interests, and values

A surefire mutual purpose: seek mutual understanding

Mutual Respect

The Continuance Condition

Others perceive that you respect them as human beings

Respect is like air—when it's gone, it's all people think about

Discussion Break #1

Think of a time when you felt unsafe in a conversation. Which condition was violated—mutual purpose or mutual respect? How did you know, and what happened to the dialogue once that condition broke down?

Four Skills to Build Safety

Use these to build safety up front or rebuild when it's lost

1. Share Your Good Intent

State what you want for everyone

2. Apologize When Appropriate

Express sorrow for your role

3. Contrast (Don't/Do)

Clarify what you don't and do intend

4. Create Mutual Purpose (CRIB)

When truly at cross purposes

Share Good Intent & Apologize

Share Your Good Intent

It's not enough to have good intentions—the other person must know that's the case.

“Our relationship is the most important thing in the world to me. Could we talk about what happened?”

Apologize When Appropriate

When you've clearly violated respect, sincerely express sorrow for your role. You have to give up saving face, being right, or winning.

“I'm sorry I didn't give you a call. You worked all night, and I didn't even explain what happened. I apologize.”

Contrast to Fix Misunderstandings

A don't/do statement that addresses concerns about your intent:

Don't Part

Address concerns that you don't respect them or have malicious purpose

“I don't want you to think I'm criticizing you...”

Do Part

Clarify what your real intention is

“I do want us to talk about our concerns so we can build our relationship.”

Pro tip: Don't wait until safety breaks—use contrasting preventively to set expectations at the start of tough conversations.

Create Mutual Purpose: CRIB

When you're truly at cross purposes

Commit to seek mutual purpose

“I commit to stay in this discussion until we find something that serves both of us.”

Recognize the purpose behind the strategy

Ask WHY they want what they're asking for. Separate demands from underlying purpose.

Invent a mutual purpose

If still at odds, find a higher or longer-term purpose that's more motivating.

Brainstorm new strategies

With mutual purpose clear, jointly search for solutions that serve everyone.

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #1)

Mutual Purpose: The entrance condition. If others believe you're working toward a common outcome and care about their interests, they'll feel safe enough to begin. A surefire starting purpose: seek mutual understanding.

Mutual Respect: The continuance condition. Even with shared purpose, if people feel disrespected, dialogue stops and becomes about defending dignity. Respect is like air—invisible until it's gone.

Key insight: People don't become defensive about what you say—they become defensive about why they think you're saying it. Safety is about perceived intent.

Key Points

  • When you feel unsafe yourself, notice which condition is at risk—that tells you which skill to use
  • You can respect someone's basic humanity even when you disagree with their behavior
  • Step out of content to rebuild safety, then step back in

Dan's Digest

Answer Key (Discussion Break #2)

Share Good Intent: Start by making your positive intentions explicit. Don't assume people know your motives.

Apologize: Requires a genuine change of heart—giving up saving face or being right to focus on what you really want.

Contrast: A don't/do statement. Address fears first (don't), then clarify real intent (do). Works even better as prevention than repair.

CRIB: For true cross-purposes. Most arguments are about strategies, not purposes. Ask WHY to find the purpose behind the demand—then brainstorm strategies that serve both purposes.

Key Points

  • Skills 1–3 handle most safety issues; Skill 4 (CRIB) is for genuine disagreements
  • Contrasting is NOT an apology—it's a clarification of intent
  • The “Write It Twice” rule for virtual communication: first for content, then reread imagining their face
