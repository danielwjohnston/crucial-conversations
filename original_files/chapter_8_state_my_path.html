Chapter 8

STATE My Path

How to speak persuasively, not abrasively

"Outspoken by whom?" — Dorothy Parker

The Challenge

When both stakes and emotions rise, we often express our views in ways that provoke defensiveness and resistance.

The more important the discussion, the less likely we are to be on our best behavior.

Most People Alternate Between:

  • Bluntly dumping their ideas ("You're not going to like this, but...")
  • Saying nothing at all out of fear
  • Sugar-coating their message so much it loses impact

The Goal

Be Totally Candid AND Completely Respectful

The best at dialogue speak their minds completely and do it in a way that makes it safe for others to hear and respond.

They are both 100% candid and 100% respectful.

How? By finding a way to maintain safety without compromising candor.

Three Essential Ingredients

To speak honestly when honesty could offend:

1

Confidence

Your opinions deserve to be in the pool of meaning

You can speak openly without brutalizing others

2

Humility

You don't have a monopoly on the truth

You're willing to change your mind with new information

3

Skill

You've found a path for both candor and safety

Your skill comes from practice and repetition

Follow the Path to Action

Share your view from beginning to end

1. See and Hear

Concrete experiences

2. Tell a Story

The meaning you make

3. Feel

Emotions generated

4. Act

Behaviors that follow

Start with facts, not your story!

When drunk on adrenaline, we start with stories and emotions—the most controversial and insulting way to begin.

The STATE Model

S – Share your facts

Start with least controversial, most persuasive elements

T – Tell your story

Explain what you're beginning to conclude

A – Ask for others' paths

Encourage others to share their facts and stories

T – Talk tentatively

State your story as a story, not as fact

E – Encourage testing

Make it safe to express differing views

Share Your Facts

Start at the source—with concrete evidence

Why Start with Facts?

  • Facts are least controversial – Less likely to spark offense
  • Facts provide context – They lay groundwork for conclusions
  • Facts demonstrate reasonableness – Show how a rational person reached your conclusion

Example Comparison

Fact: "You arrived at 8:20 AM"

Story: "You were 20 minutes late"

Judgment: "You can't be trusted"

Why Facts Matter

  • Facts form the foundation for the conversation
  • Facts are less offensive than jumping to conclusions
  • Facts help others see how a reasonable person reached your conclusion
  • Facts prevent others from telling villain stories about you

When you start with shocking conclusions, people are left to make up reasons you're saying them—they're likely to believe you're either stupid or evil.

Tell Your Story

After sharing facts, explain your conclusion

Key Points:

  • Don't start with your story—earn the right by sharing facts first
  • Tell it tentatively as a possible story, not proven fact
  • Don't pile on—avoid unloading your entire arsenal of judgments
  • Watch for safety problems as you share

Example

"Since I started working here, you've asked to meet with me twice a day—more than anyone else. You've also asked me to pass all my ideas by you first. I'm not sure you're intending this, but I'm beginning to wonder if you don't trust me. Is that what's going on?"

Why Share Your Story?

  • Facts alone are rarely worth mentioning
  • It's facts + conclusion that call for discussion
  • If you only mention facts, others may not understand the severity
  • Sharing your story shows you've thought through the implications

It Takes Confidence

Sharing potentially inflammatory conclusions (like "I think you're a thief") requires confidence.

But if you've done your homework by thinking through the facts, you'll realize you're drawing a reasonable, rational, and decent conclusion—one that deserves to be heard.

Ask for Others' Paths

Blend confidence with humility

Once you've shared your facts and stories clearly, invite others to do the same.

Open-Ended Questions

"How do you see it?"

"What's your perspective?"

"Can you help me understand?"

Be willing to abandon or reshape your story as more information pours into the pool of meaning.

Your goal is to achieve valued results, not to gratify your ego by being right.

Talk Tentatively

Tell your story as a story, not as fact

Tentative language:

  • "I'm beginning to conclude that..."
  • "I'm tempted to think..."
  • "In my opinion..."
  • "Perhaps you were unaware..."
  • "I don't think it will work..."

Strike a blend between confidence and humility—share in a way that expresses appropriate confidence while demonstrating willingness to be challenged.

Tentative vs. Wimpy

❌ Wimpy

"I know this is probably not true..."

"Call me crazy, but..."

"I'm probably wrong..."

Acts clinically uncertain

✓ Tentative

"I'm beginning to wonder if..."

"It appears to me that..."

"I'm starting to feel like..."

Shares opinion, not nervousness

Important: Being tentative is not being manipulative. You should speak tentatively because you're actually not certain your opinions represent absolute truth.

The Irony of Dialogue

A Counterintuitive Truth

When there's a difference of opinions:

The more convinced and forceful you act,
the more resistant others become.

Speaking in absolute and overstated terms does not increase your influence—it decreases it.

The converse is also true: The more tentatively you speak, the more open people become to your opinions.

Encourage Testing

Make it safe to disagree with you

The only limit to how strongly you can express your opinion is your willingness to be equally vigorous in encouraging others to challenge it.

Ways to Encourage Testing:

  • Invite opposing views: "Does anyone see it differently?"
  • Be genuine: Make sure your invitation doesn't sound like a threat
  • Play devil's advocate: "Maybe I'm wrong here. What if the opposite is true?"
  • Persist: Continue encouraging until your openness becomes obvious

The Goldilocks Test

Finding the right balance

Not too soft, not too harsh—just right!

Example 1: Suspected Theft

Understated

"This is probably stupid, but..."

Overstated

"How come you ripped us off?"

Just Right

"It appears you're taking this home for your own use. Is that right?"

More Goldilocks Examples

Example 2: Drug Use Concern

Understated

"I'm ashamed to even mention this..."

Overstated

"When did you start using hard drugs?"

Just Right

"This is leading me to conclude you're using drugs. Do you have another explanation?"

Example 3: Trust Issues

Understated

"It's probably my fault, but..."

Overstated

"You wouldn't trust your own mother!"

Just Right

"I'm starting to feel like you don't trust me. Is that what's going on?"

The Missing Money

Anita's story demonstrates STATE in action

The Situation

Anita planned to use $40 from her wallet to pay for groceries. The money was gone. Her 16-year-old daughter Amber had asked for money the night before, was told no, but went out anyway.

The Wrong Way

"I can't believe you would steal from me! You want to spend the next decade in your room?"

The Missing Money - Done Right

Share Facts

"Amber, when I went to pay just now, I was planning to use $40 I thought I had in my wallet. But when I opened it, the money wasn't there. I saw it yesterday. Then I remembered you asking for money last night. I told you no, and you went out anyway."

Tell Story (Tentatively)

"Obviously, one possibility is that you took the money."

Ask for Others' Path

"Honestly, I don't know what to think. All I know is what I just shared, and I hope you can see how I might at least have the question. Did you?"

The Missing Money - Result

Contrast (When Needed)

"Amber, honey. I know you're a good kid, and I don't want to jump to hurtful conclusions. I also know people make mistakes—I did when I was your age. I want us to be able to talk about things, even hard things, honestly and openly."

The Outcome

Amber admitted: "I was planning to put it back. I wasn't trying to steal it. I didn't think you'd notice before I got my paycheck today."

They talked about it. There were consequences. But Anita also learned about the pressures Amber was facing and gained influence in her daughter's life.

When Strong Belief Weakens Influence

The problem with being absolutely certain

When you're sure you're right and others are wrong:

  • You feel no need to expand the pool of meaning
  • You believe it's your duty to fight for truth
  • You start using debate tricks and dirty tactics
  • The harder you try, the greater the resistance you create

We appeal to authority, attack the person, draw hasty generalizations, attack straw men—all while creating more and more resistance.

The Solution

When you're dying to convince others you're right

Step 1: Back off and think

What do I really want—for myself, others, and the relationship?

Step 2: Ask yourself

What should I do right now to move toward what I really want?

Step 3: Check your behavior

Are you leaning forward? Speaking louder? Trying to win?

The harder it is for you to use STATE skills, the more likely your goal is to WIN rather than LEARN.

Practice This Week

  • Plan tentative language: Practice saying "I'm beginning to wonder..." instead of "You always..."
  • Ask genuine questions: Invite others' perspectives with curiosity
  • Watch for your own certainty: When you feel absolutely right, pause and use STATE

Remember

Facts First

Start with observable evidence, not your conclusions. Let others travel your path with you.

Stay Curious

The goal is to achieve valued results, not to be right. Be willing to revise your story.

Confidence + Humility + Skill = Effective Dialogue

You can be both totally candid and completely respectful.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Starting with your story instead of facts
  • Piling on by sharing every negative conclusion you've drawn
  • Being wimpy instead of appropriately tentative
  • Asking fake questions that are really accusations
  • Not really wanting to hear the other person's perspective
  • Defending your story instead of being open to changing it

Your willingness to use STATE skills is a reliable indicator of whether you want dialogue or just to win.

Key Takeaway

STATE Your Path

When you have a tough message to share, or when you're so convinced of your rightness that you may push too hard:

Share your facts. Tell your story. Ask for others' paths. Talk tentatively. Encourage testing.

Follow the Path to Action from beginning to end. Help others see how a reasonable, rational, and decent person could end up with the story you're carrying.
