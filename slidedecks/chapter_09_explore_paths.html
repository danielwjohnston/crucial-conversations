How to Listen When Others Blow Up or Clam Up

"One of the best ways to persuade others is with your ears - by listening to them."
— Dean Rusk

Sanj's Failed Attempt

Let's look at a common scenario where dialogue fails:

The Scene:

"What risks do you all see in the current project plan?" Sanj asks.

He looks at the team assembled around the table and sees blank face after blank face. A few people are looking down, diligently concentrating on their doodling. Others meet his eyes for a moment before looking away. No one says anything.

Sanj's Response:

"I think we all know how important this project is. That's why we're all here. If we're going to succeed, we have to be able to talk about the risks in the plan so that we can mitigate them. What are your concerns?"

More silence.

"Okay. Well, then great. That's just great." He says, with obvious sarcasm.

What Went Wrong?

Sanj made several common mistakes that shut down dialogue:

The Problems:

  • He didn't notice the silence - People were clearly uncomfortable
  • He pushed harder - Instead of stepping back, he doubled down
  • He became sarcastic - This made people even less likely to share
  • He focused on his goal - Not on the team's safety

When people clam up or blow up, the first step is to restore safety, not push harder.

The Four Power Listening Skills

When others are in silence or violence mode, use these four skills to encourage dialogue:

1. Ask

Use genuine, open-ended questions to encourage sharing.

2. Mirror

Reflect back what you see and hear to show you're paying attention.

3. Paraphrase

Restate what you believe they're saying in your own words.

4. Prime

Offer your best guess at what they might be thinking or feeling.

Ask - Genuine Questions

Ask questions that show genuine curiosity, not interrogation.

Good Questions:

  • "What's your take on this?"
  • "How do you feel about this situation?"
  • "What are you thinking?"
  • "Can you help me understand your perspective?"
  • "What would you like to see happen here?"

Avoid:

  • "Why are you so quiet?" (accusatory)
  • "Don't you have anything to say?" (pressuring)
  • "What's wrong with you?" (judgmental)

Mirror - Reflect What You See

Mirror the facts and emotions you observe to show you're paying attention.

Examples:

  • "You seem hesitant to share your thoughts."
  • "I notice you've been quiet during our discussion."
  • "You look concerned about this proposal."
  • "It sounds like you're frustrated with the current situation."
  • "You seem to have strong feelings about this."

Mirroring isn't about mimicking—it's about reflecting back what you observe to show understanding.

Paraphrase and Prime

Paraphrase:

Restate what you believe they're saying in your own words.

  • "So what you're saying is..."
  • "If I understand correctly..."
  • "Let me see if I've got this right..."

Prime:

Offer your best guess at what they might be thinking or feeling.

  • "Are you thinking that..."
  • "I wonder if you're concerned about..."
  • "Could it be that you're worried about..."

Practice:

How could Sanj have used these skills differently? What would you have said in his situation?
