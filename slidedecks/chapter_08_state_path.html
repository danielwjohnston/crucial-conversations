How to Speak Persuasively, Not Abrasively

"Outspoken, by whom?"
— Dorothy Parker, when told she was very outspoken

We're Ready to Speak

So far, we've gone to great pains to prepare ourselves to step up to and master crucial conversations.

Here's what we've learned:

  • Our hearts need to be in the right place
  • Our heads need to be focused on the right topic
  • We need to let go of clever stories that keep us stuck
  • We need to learn to look for safety problems
  • We need to restore safety when necessary

Now we're actually going to express our opinion. But how do we do it without creating defensiveness?

The Challenge: Sharing Strong Views

What most of the time we do when we share strong opinions is:

We move from silence to violence:

  • We start with our conclusions
  • We use absolute terms ("always," "never")
  • We overstate our case
  • We blame others
  • We shut down dialogue

The Result:

People become defensive, stop listening, and the conversation fails.

The STATE Method

To share your strong views without creating defensiveness, use the STATE method:

S - Share your facts

Start with the least controversial, most persuasive elements: facts.

T - Tell your story

Explain what you think, feel, or believe based on your facts.

A - Ask for others' paths

Invite others to share their facts, stories, and conclusions.

T - Talk tentatively

Use softening language and acknowledge uncertainty.

E - Encourage testing

Make it safe for others to challenge or disagree with you.

S - Share Your Facts

Start with the least controversial, most persuasive elements: facts.

What makes a good fact?

  • Observable - Others can see or verify it
  • Neutral - No judgment or emotion
  • Relevant - Directly related to your point
  • Complete - Not cherry-picked

Instead of:

"You're always late to meetings and don't respect my time."

Try:

"In the last three team meetings, you've arrived 10-15 minutes after the scheduled start time."

T - Tell Your Story

Explain what you think, feel, or believe based on your facts.

Connect facts to conclusions:

Help others understand how you got from the facts to your conclusions.

Example:

"When you arrive late to meetings (fact), I feel like my time isn't valued (feeling), and I'm concerned that we're not making the best use of our limited time together (conclusion)."

Remember:

Your story is your interpretation of the facts. Others may have different interpretations.

A - Ask for Others' Paths

Invite others to share their facts, stories, and conclusions.

Why this matters:

  • Shows you're interested in dialogue, not monologue
  • Demonstrates respect for their perspective
  • Creates safety for them to share
  • Helps you understand their thinking

Good questions:

  • "How do you see it?"
  • "What am I missing?"
  • "What's your perspective on this?"
  • "Can you help me understand your thinking?"

T - Talk Tentatively

Use softening language and acknowledge uncertainty.

Avoid:

  • "The fact is..."
  • "Obviously..."
  • "Everyone knows..."
  • "You always..."
  • "You never..."

Try:

  • "In my opinion..."
  • "I'm wondering if..."
  • "It seems to me that..."
  • "I could be wrong, but..."
  • "Help me understand..."

Tentative language doesn't weaken your position—it strengthens it by making it more approachable.

E - Encourage Testing

Make it safe for others to challenge or disagree with you.

How to encourage testing:

  • Explicitly invite disagreement
  • Show genuine curiosity
  • Thank people for different views
  • Acknowledge when they make good points
  • Be willing to change your mind

Examples:

"I might be missing something here. What do you think?"

"I'd really like to hear your perspective, even if it's different from mine."

"If you see this differently, please let me know. I want to understand."

When people feel safe to challenge you, they're more likely to listen to you.
