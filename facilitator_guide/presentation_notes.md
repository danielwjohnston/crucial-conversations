# Facilitator Presentation Notes

## General Guidelines

### Opening Each Session

1. **Welcome and Ground Rules** (5 minutes)
   - Thank participants for their time and commitment
   - Establish psychological safety
   - Set ground rules for discussion:
     - Confidentiality
     - Respectful listening
     - No judgment
     - Participation is voluntary but encouraged

2. **Learning Objectives** (3 minutes)
   - Review what participants will learn
   - Connect to their real-world challenges
   - Emphasize practical application

3. **Agenda Overview** (2 minutes)
   - Brief outline of the session
   - Timing and break schedule
   - Materials and resources

### During Presentations

- **Pace yourself**: Speak slowly and clearly
- **Use the slides**: Don't read them, but use them as guides
- **Encourage participation**: Ask questions regularly
- **Watch the room**: Monitor energy and engagement
- **Be flexible**: Adjust based on group needs

### Closing Each Session

1. **Key Takeaways** (5 minutes)
   - Summarize main points
   - Ask participants what they learned
   - Connect to next session

2. **Action Planning** (5 minutes)
   - Specific commitments for practice
   - How they'll apply the skills
   - Support needed

3. **Next Steps** (3 minutes)
   - Homework assignments
   - Resources for further learning
   - Date/time of next session

## Chapter-Specific Notes

### Chapter 1: What's a Crucial Conversation

#### Ch 1 — Key Points

- **Definition**: High stakes, opinions vary, emotions run strong
- **Impact**: These conversations determine success in relationships and results
- **Hope**: Skills are learnable, not innate

#### Ch 1 — Discussion Prompt

- "Think about a crucial conversation you've been avoiding — one where the stakes are high, opinions differ, and emotions run strong. What's the cost of continuing to avoid it, and what would it look like if you handled it well?"

#### Ch 1 — Activity Ideas

- **Group Brainstorm**: Common workplace crucial conversations — what makes them crucial?
- **Whole-Group Scenario Walk-Through**: Facilitator presents a scenario and the group identifies the three elements together
- **Role Play**: Simple scenarios to practice recognition

#### Ch 1 — Common Challenges

- Participants may feel overwhelmed by the definition
- Some may think "this doesn't apply to me"
- Solution: Use relatable examples and stories

#### Ch 1 — Facilitator Tips

- Share a personal crucial conversation story
- Use humor to reduce anxiety
- Emphasize that everyone has these conversations

### Chapter 2: Mastering Crucial Conversations

#### Ch 2 — Key Points

- **Research-based**: These skills come from studying real experts
- **Not magic**: Deliberate practice, not natural talent
- **Universal**: Apply across all contexts

#### Ch 2 — Discussion Prompt

- "Think about someone you've seen handle a high-stakes conversation with real skill — what specifically did they do that made it work, and what would change in your own life or work if you could do the same?"

#### Ch 2 — Activity Ideas

- **Skill Assessment**: Participants rate their current abilities and share insights with the group
- **Whole-Group Expert Analysis**: Together, identify what makes someone skilled at dialogue
- **Commitment Planning**: Group discussion on how they'll practice

#### Ch 2 — Common Challenges

- Skepticism about the research
- Belief that some people are just "naturally good"
- Solution: Emphasize the learning process

#### Ch 2 — Facilitator Tips

- Show before/after examples
- Emphasize that everyone starts somewhere
- Create hope and motivation

### Chapter 3: Choose Your Topic

#### Ch 3 — Key Points

- **Topic selection matters**: Wrong topic = wrong conversation
- **CPR method**: Content, Pattern, Relationship
- **Surface vs. real issues**: Look deeper

#### Ch 3 — Discussion Prompt

- "Think about a recurring frustration at work or home — is the real issue the specific Content of what happened, a Pattern you've noticed over time, or something deeper about the Relationship itself? How would your approach change depending on which level you address?"

#### Ch 3 — Activity Ideas

- **CPR Practice**: Group works through real situations together, applying CPR analysis
- **Topic Sorting**: Facilitator presents scenarios and the group categorizes by CPR level
- **Role Play**: Practice topic identification with whole-group debrief

#### Ch 3 — Common Challenges

- Participants want to jump to solutions
- Difficulty distinguishing between CPR levels
- Solution: Use clear examples and practice

#### Ch 3 — Facilitator Tips

- Use workplace examples
- Emphasize the diagnostic process
- Validate that this takes practice

### Chapter 4: Start with Heart

#### Ch 4 — Key Points

- **Three questions**: Self, others, relationship
- **Purpose clarification**: Know what you really want
- **Emotional regulation**: Purpose over emotions

#### Ch 4 — Discussion Prompt

- "Think about a time when emotions hijacked a conversation and you lost sight of what you actually wanted — what was the trigger, and looking back, what did you really want for yourself, for the other person, and for the relationship?"

#### Ch 4 — Activity Ideas

- **Purpose Clarification**: Group walks through the three questions together using a shared scenario
- **Emotional Triggers**: Whole-group discussion of common triggers and how to recognize them in the moment
- **Planning Sheet**: Participants prepare for upcoming conversations, then share key insights with the group

#### Ch 4 — Common Challenges

- Participants struggle with the "what I want for others" question
- Some can't identify their real purpose
- Solution: Guide them through the process gently

#### Ch 4 — Facilitator Tips

- Share your own struggles with this
- Emphasize that this is the foundation
- Be patient with the process

### Chapter 5: Master My Stories

#### Ch 5 — Key Points

- **Path to Action**: See/Hear → Tell a Story → Feel → Act
- **Stories create feelings**: Change the story, change the emotion
- **Three clever stories**: Victim, Villain, Helpless

#### Ch 5 — Discussion Prompt

- "Think about a recent situation where your emotions got the better of you — what story were you telling yourself, and how might retelling that story with more honesty about your own role change how you feel and respond?"

#### Ch 5 — Activity Ideas

- **Path to Action Mapping**: Group traces a real scenario through all four steps together
- **Clever Story Identification**: Facilitator presents examples and the group identifies which clever story is at play
- **Story Retelling**: Whole-group exercise rewriting a Victim, Villain, or Helpless story into a more useful one

#### Ch 5 — Common Challenges

- Participants resist the idea that they create their own emotions
- Difficulty separating facts from stories
- Solution: Use humor and relatable examples to make the concept accessible

#### Ch 5 — Facilitator Tips

- Emphasize this is about leverage, not blame
- Share a personal "clever story" example
- Normalize the difficulty of this skill

### Chapter 6: Learn to Look

#### Ch 6 — Key Points

- **Signs of silence**: Masking, avoiding, withdrawing
- **Signs of violence**: Controlling, labeling, attacking
- **Dual processing**: Watch content AND conditions simultaneously

#### Ch 6 — Discussion Prompt

- "When conversations go sideways at work, what are the earliest warning signs you notice — in yourself and in others — that safety is at risk, and what typically happens if those signs go unaddressed?"

#### Ch 6 — Activity Ideas

- **Signal Spotting**: Facilitator describes scenarios and the group identifies silence vs. violence cues
- **Self-Awareness Inventory**: Group discusses their own default patterns under stress
- **Video Analysis**: If available, watch a clip and identify the moment safety breaks down

#### Ch 6 — Common Challenges

- Participants may only recognize obvious signs (yelling) but miss subtle ones (withdrawal)
- Some struggle to monitor themselves while in a conversation
- Solution: Practice with low-stakes examples first

#### Ch 6 — Facilitator Tips

- Use body language demonstrations
- Emphasize that noticing is the first step, not fixing
- Connect to Chapter 5 — stories drive the reactions you're now learning to spot

### Chapter 7: Make It Safe

#### Ch 7 — Key Points

- **Mutual Purpose**: Do they believe I care about their goals?
- **Mutual Respect**: Do they believe I respect them as a person?
- **CRIB skills**: Commit, Recognize, Invent, Brainstorm
- **Contrasting**: Address misunderstandings with "I don't want… / I do want…"

#### Ch 7 — Discussion Prompt

- "Think about a conversation that derailed because the other person felt disrespected or misunderstood your intent — what would it have looked like to step out of the content and rebuild safety before continuing?"

#### Ch 7 — Activity Ideas

- **Contrasting Practice**: Group creates "I don't want / I do want" statements for common workplace scenarios
- **CRIB Walk-Through**: Facilitator leads the group through all four steps on a shared example
- **Safety Repair Role Play**: Whole-group demonstration of stepping out to rebuild safety

#### Ch 7 — Common Challenges

- Participants confuse "making it safe" with "being nice" or avoiding the issue
- Contrasting feels awkward at first
- Solution: Emphasize that safety enables honesty, it doesn't replace it

#### Ch 7 — Facilitator Tips

- Demonstrate contrasting live — it resonates when they see it
- Stress that this is the most revisited skill in real conversations
- Connect back to Start with Heart — safety starts with your intent

### Chapter 8: STATE My Path

#### Ch 8 — Key Points

- **S**hare your facts
- **T**ell your story
- **A**sk for others' paths
- **T**alk tentatively
- **E**ncourage testing
- **Facts first**: Facts are the least controversial and most persuasive starting point

#### Ch 8 — Discussion Prompt

- "Think about a conversation you need to have where you hold a strong opinion — how would you share that opinion starting with the observable facts, telling your story tentatively, and then genuinely inviting the other person to share their perspective?"

#### Ch 8 — Activity Ideas

- **STATE Construction**: Group builds a complete STATE statement together for a real-world scenario
- **Tentative Language Practice**: Facilitator presents blunt statements and the group reframes them using tentative language
- **Fact vs. Story Sorting**: Group distinguishes facts from stories in a series of examples

#### Ch 8 — Common Challenges

- Participants may equate "tentative" with "weak"
- Difficulty separating facts from interpretations
- Solution: Emphasize that tentativeness invites dialogue, it doesn't undermine your point

#### Ch 8 — Facilitator Tips

- Model the STATE framework live with a real example
- Emphasize that "Talk tentatively" is about confidence with humility
- This chapter pairs directly with Chapter 5 — facts are what you saw, stories are what you added

### Chapter 9: Explore Others' Paths

#### Ch 9 — Key Points

- **AMPP skills**: Ask, Mirror, Paraphrase, Prime
- **Curiosity over defensiveness**: Genuinely seek to understand
- **ABC method**: Agree, Build, Compare when you disagree

#### Ch 9 — Discussion Prompt

- "When someone shares a view you strongly disagree with, what's your natural response — and how would it change the conversation if you genuinely explored their path first, using curiosity instead of counterarguments?"

#### Ch 9 — Activity Ideas

- **AMPP Practice**: Facilitator plays a frustrated stakeholder and the group practices each AMPP skill
- **ABC Response Building**: Group takes a disagreement scenario and crafts Agree, Build, and Compare responses
- **Listening Demonstration**: Facilitator models the difference between defensive listening and curious listening

#### Ch 9 — Common Challenges

- Participants may feel that listening = agreeing
- Priming feels manipulative to some
- Solution: Emphasize that understanding someone's path doesn't mean accepting their conclusion

#### Ch 9 — Facilitator Tips

- Priming is for when someone is stuck, not a default tool — clarify that
- Model ABC explicitly — most people default to "Compare" and skip "Agree" and "Build"
- Connect to Make It Safe — exploring paths is how you maintain safety during disagreement

### Chapter 10: Retake Your Pen

#### Ch 10 — Key Points

- **Authoring your story**: You choose how others' actions affect you
- **Separating fact from story**: Others act; you write the meaning
- **Emotional resilience**: Stop giving away your emotional pen

#### Ch 10 — Discussion Prompt

- "Who in your life have you given your pen to — meaning whose words or actions have an outsized power to determine how you feel — and what would it look like to take that pen back while still caring about the relationship?"

#### Ch 10 — Activity Ideas

- **Pen Audit**: Group identifies their top "pen holders" and discusses what gives those people power
- **Rewrite Exercise**: Facilitator presents a triggering scenario and the group rewrites the story without giving away the pen
- **Resilience Strategies**: Whole-group brainstorm of practical techniques for maintaining authorship

#### Ch 10 — Common Challenges

- Participants may confuse "retaking the pen" with not caring
- Some may feel this minimizes real harm from others
- Solution: Emphasize that this is about choosing your response, not excusing bad behavior

#### Ch 10 — Facilitator Tips

- This is deeply personal — create extra safety before diving in
- Use the metaphor consistently — it resonates
- Connect to Master My Stories — this is the advanced version of that skill

### Chapter 11: Move to Action

#### Ch 11 — Key Points

- **Decision methods**: Command, Consult, Vote, Consensus
- **Accountability elements**: Who, What, When, Follow-up
- **From dialogue to results**: Action is the goal

#### Ch 11 — Discussion Prompt

- "Think about a time when your team had a productive conversation but nothing actually changed afterward — what was missing in terms of how the decision was made and who owned the follow-through?"

#### Ch 11 — Activity Ideas

- **Method Selection**: Facilitator presents scenarios and the group decides which decision method fits best
- **Accountability Planning**: Group works through the four elements (Who, What, When, Follow-up) on a shared example
- **Action Mapping**: Whole-group exercise mapping a conversation to concrete results

#### Ch 11 — Common Challenges

- Participants focus only on the decision method
- Forget the accountability piece
- Solution: Emphasize both components equally

#### Ch 11 — Facilitator Tips

- Use real workplace decisions as examples
- Emphasize that clarity prevents problems
- Connect back to earlier skills

- **Universal application**: Skills work everywhere
- **More effort, not different skills**: Better execution

#### Ch 12 — Discussion Prompt

- "What's your strongest 'yeah, but' — the situation where you feel these skills just won't work — and what would it take to apply even one principle from this program to move that situation forward?"

#### Ch 12 — Activity Ideas

- **Solution Brainstorming**: Whole-group effort to address the toughest cases that come up
- **Strategy Planning**: Group develops strategies for handling the most common tough cases

#### Ch 12 — Common Challenges

- Participants may feel hopeless about their situations
- Some may want to debate specific scenarios
- Solution: Acknowledge difficulty while emphasizing possibility

#### Ch 12 — Facilitator Tips

- Validate their challenges
- Share success stories from similar situations
- Emphasize that progress takes time

## General Facilitation Techniques

### Questioning Strategies

1. **Open-ended questions**: Encourage detailed responses
2. **Follow-up questions**: Dig deeper into understanding
3. **Connecting questions**: Link to previous points
4. **Application questions**: Focus on real-world use

### Managing Group Dynamics

1. **Dominant speakers**: "Thanks for that contribution. Let's hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet."
2. **Quiet participants**: "John, you looked thoughtful. What are you thinking?"
3. **Side conversations**: Address gently and redirect
4. **Difficult questions**: Validate, then involve the group

### Time Management

1. **Use visible timers**: Keep activities on track
2. **Have a parking lot**: Capture important off-topic items
3. **Build in flexibility**: Know what can be shortened
4. **Energy management**: Watch for fatigue and adjust

### Creating Safety

1. **Validate contributions**: "That's a great point."
2. **Normalize struggles**: "Many people find that challenging."
3. **Share appropriately**: Use personal stories judiciously
4. **Model vulnerability**: Admit your own challenges

## Handling Common Situations

### Technical Difficulties

1. **Have backups**: Printed materials, alternative activities
2. **Stay calm**: Your reaction sets the tone
3. **Use it as learning**: Model problem-solving
4. **Have a plan**: Know what to do if technology fails

### Challenging Participants

1. **The Skeptic**: Acknowledge concerns, invite examples
2. **The Expert**: Validate knowledge, redirect to group learning
3. **The Victim**: Empathize, focus on what they can control
4. **The Multitasker**: Engage directly, make it relevant

### Energy Issues

1. **Low energy**: More interactive activities, movement
2. **High energy**: Use it for practice, channel productively
3. **Distraction**: Short breaks, change of pace
4. **Fatigue**: Acknowledge, adjust expectations

## Troubleshooting Guide

### Time Running Behind

1. **Identify flexible content**: What can be shortened?
2. **Combine activities**: Merge discussion and practice
3. **Use parking lot**: Move less critical items
4. **Adjust breaks**: Shorten but don't eliminate

### Low Participation

1. **Written response first**: Give time to process before asking for whole-group contributions
2. **Warm-up questions**: Start with low-stakes, relatable prompts before deeper questions
3. **Direct invitation**: "Sarah, you looked thoughtful — what's on your mind?" (non-threatening)
4. **Facilitator modeling**: Share your own experience first to normalize vulnerability

### Content Too Advanced

1. **More examples**: Concrete illustrations
2. **Step-by-step**: Break down complex concepts
3. **Practice time**: Apply immediately
4. **Simplify language**: Avoid jargon

### Content Too Basic

1. **Advanced scenarios**: More complex situations
2. **Peer teaching**: Participants share expertise
3. **Application focus**: Real workplace challenges
4. **Deeper analysis**: Go beyond surface level

## Self-Care for Facilitators

### Before Sessions

- **Review materials**: Know your content
- **Prepare mentally**: Center yourself
- **Set up early**: Avoid last-minute stress
- **Have water**: Stay hydrated

### During Sessions

- **Monitor your energy**: Take care of yourself
- **Use your co-facilitator**: Share the load
- **Take breaks**: Even short ones help
- **Stay present**: Focus on current moment

### After Sessions

- **Debrief**: What went well? What to change?
- **Rest**: Recover your energy
- **Document**: Notes for next time
- **Celebrate**: Acknowledge your effort
