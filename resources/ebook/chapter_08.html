Part II — How to Open Your Mouth
Chapter 8

STATE My Path

How to Speak Persuasively, Not Abrasively
Listen to this chapter

Brian has a problem. He thinks his boss, Fernando, is micromanaging him to within an inch of his life. He says the same thing over lunch when talking with his pals. Everyone knows what Brian thinks about Fernando—except, of course, Fernando.

People who are skilled at dialogue have the confidence to say what needs to be said to the person who needs to hear it. They are confident that their opinions deserve to be placed in the pool of meaning, and that they can speak openly without brutalizing others or causing undue offense.

Humility. Confidence does not equate to arrogance or pigheadedness. Skilled people realize that they don’t have a monopoly on the truth. Their opinions provide a starting point but not the final word. This means they’re willing to both express their opinions and encourage others to do the same.

Skill. People who willingly share delicate information are good at doing it. They don’t make a Fool’s Choice, because they’ve found a path that allows for both candor and safety.

The Missing Money

To see how to discuss sensitive issues, let’s look at a difficult problem. Anita has just opened her wallet at the checkout stand—no money. She immediately turns to her 16-year-old daughter standing next to her and barks: “Amber! Where is it?” It took Anita all of half a second to jump from “I thought I had $40 in here” to “She rifled through my wallet and took my cash!” How should she talk about her worrisome conclusion in a way that leads to dialogue?

STATE My Path

Start with Heart. Think about what you really want and how dialogue can help you get it. Master your story—realize you may be jumping to a hasty Victim, Villain, or Helpless Story. Then draw upon five distinct skills that can help you talk about even the most sensitive topics. These five tools are remembered with the acronym STATE:

  • Share your facts.
  • Tell your story.
  • Ask for others’ paths.
  • Talk tentatively.
  • Encourage testing.

The first three skills describe what to do. The last two tell how to do it.

The “What” Skills

The best way to share your view is to follow the Path to Action model from Chapter 5, from beginning to end. Starting with our ugly stories is the most controversial, least influential, and most insulting way we could begin.

Figure 8.1 Path to Action: SEE & HEAR → TELL A STORY → FEEL → ACT

Share Your Facts

The first step is to retrace your Path to Action to the source and find the facts—concrete evidence such as things you have seen, heard, or experienced directly.

Facts are the least controversial. Facts provide a safe beginning. “Yesterday you arrived at work at 8:20 a.m.” is a fact. “You were 20 minutes late” adds story. “You can’t be trusted” is hardly a fact. Start with the least controversial, most persuasive elements. Which beginning is less offensive?

“Stop sexually harassing me!”

or

“When you talk to me, your eyes move up and down rather than look at my face. And sometimes you put your hand on my shoulder.”

Facts form the foundation for the conversation. They become the starting point and are less likely to spark offense. Gathering the facts is the homework required for Crucial Conversations.

Remember that you are sharing your facts. When you acknowledge these are your facts, you make space for other facts—things the other person may have seen and heard.

Tell Your Story

Don’t start with your story. Let them experience your path from the beginning to the end. Starting with your facts, followed by your story. When you do talk about what you’re starting to conclude, tell it as a possible story, not as proven fact.

Brian (facts): Since I started work here, you’ve asked to meet with me twice a day. That’s more than with anyone else. You’ve also asked me to pass all my ideas by you before I include them in a project.

Brian (possible story): I’m not sure that you’re intending to send this message, but I’m beginning to wonder if you don’t trust me. Maybe you think that I’m not up to the job or that I’ll get you into trouble. Is that what’s going on?

Fernando: Really, I was merely trying to give you a chance to get my input before you got too far down the path on a project. I’m trying to avoid surprises.

It takes confidence. If you’ve done your homework by thinking through the facts behind your story, you’ll realize that you are drawing a reasonable, rational, and decent conclusion. One that deserves to be heard.

Don’t pile it on. Work on your Villain Story before you have any hope of healthy dialogue. Your story will begin to sound more like: “When I heard your recommendation, my initial reaction was to oppose your decision. But after thinking about it, I’ve realized I could be wrong . . . I’d like to talk about how both of us can objectively weigh this decision.”

Look for safety problems. As you share your story, watch for signs that safety is deteriorating. If people become defensive, step out and rebuild safety by Contrasting.

Ask for Others’ Paths

Once you’ve shared your point of view, invite others to do the same—and mean it. Open-ended questions encourage others to express their facts, stories, and feelings:

“How do you see it?” — “What’s your perspective?” — “Can you help me understand?”

Be willing to abandon or reshape your story as more information pours into the Pool of Shared Meaning. What you really want is to achieve valued results, not to gratify your ego by being right.

The “How” Skills

Talk Tentatively

Talking tentatively means telling your story as a story rather than disguising it as a hard fact. “Perhaps you were unaware . . .” “In my opinion . . .” “I’m beginning to conclude that . . .”

That’s a stupid idea . . .I don’t think it will work . . .

One of the ironies of dialogue is that the more convinced and forceful you act, the more resistant others become. The more tentatively you speak, the more open people become to your opinions.

Tentative, not wimpy. Don’t wimp out by acting as if your content isn’t important: “I know this is probably not true . . .” Use language that says you’re sharing an opinion, not language that says you’re a nervous wreck.

A “Good” Story—the Goldilocks Test

Understated: “This is probably stupid, but . . .”
Overstated: “How come you ripped us off?”
Just right: “It appears to me that you’re taking this home for your own use. Is that right?”

Understated: “I’m ashamed to even mention this, but . . .”
Overstated: “Just when did you start using hard drugs?”
Just right: “It’s leading me to conclude that you’re starting to use drugs. Do you have another explanation I’m missing here?”

Understated: “It’s probably my fault, but . . .”
Overstated: “You wouldn’t trust your own mother to make toast!”
Just right: “I’m starting to feel like you don’t trust me. Is that what’s going on?”

Encourage Testing

Not only should you invite others to talk, but do so in a way that makes it clear you want to hear them no matter how controversial their ideas might be.

Invite opposing views. If their views disagree with yours, so much the better. Make it clear: “I know people have been reluctant to speak up about this, but I would really love to hear from everyone.”

Mean it. Invite people with both words and tone that say, “I really want to hear from you.”

Play devil’s advocate. Model disagreeing with your own view: “Maybe I’m wrong here. What if the opposite is true? . . . I really want to hear all the reasons my position could be dangerously wrong.”

Encourage others until your motive becomes obvious. You can argue as vigorously as you want for your point of view, provided you are even more vigorous about encouraging others to disprove it. The real test of whether your motive is to win a debate or engage in real dialogue is the degree to which you encourage testing.

Back to the Missing Money

Anita (facts): Amber, when I went to pay for the groceries just now, I was planning to use $40 that I thought I had in my wallet. But when I opened my wallet, the money wasn’t there. I thought it was strange, because I saw it there yesterday. Then I remembered you asking for some money last night to go out with your friends. I told you no. And you ended up going to a movie and dinner with them anyway.

Anita (tentative story): Obviously one possibility is that you took the money.

Amber: You think I stole your money?

Anita (asks for path): Honestly? I don’t know what to think. All I know is what I just shared, and I hope you can see how I might at least have the question. Did you?

Anita (Contrasting): Amber, honey, I know you’re a good kid, and I don’t want to jump to hurtful conclusions. I also know people make mistakes. I want us to be able to talk about things, even hard things, honestly and openly.

Amber: I was planning to put it back. I wasn’t trying to steal it. I didn’t think you’d notice before I got my paycheck today.

The suspicious mother avoided nasty accusations and ugly stories, shared facts, and then tentatively shared a possible conclusion. They talked about it, there were consequences for the theft, and they also talked about the pressures that had led the daughter to take the money. She gained influence in her teenage daughter’s life that day because of how she tackled a tough conversation.

When Strong Belief Weakens Your Influence

When stakes rise and others argue differing views—and you just know in your heart of hearts that you’re right and they’re wrong—you start pushing too hard. In the end, nobody is listening, everyone is committed to silence or violence, and the Pool of Shared Meaning remains parched and tainted. Nobody wins.

It starts with a story. When we believe we’re right and everyone else is wrong, we feel no need to expand the pool of meaning. We feel justified in using dirty tricks: stacking the deck, exaggeration, inflammatory language. And the harder we try and the more forceful and nasty our tactics, the greater the resistance we create.

How Do We Change?

When you find yourself dying to convince others, back off your current attack and think about what you really want for yourself, others, and the relationship.

  • First, Learn to Look. Watch for the moment when people start to resist you. Turn your attention away from the topic and onto yourself. Remember: The more you care about an issue, the less likely you are to be on your best behavior.
  • Second, check your intent. Do you want to be heard, understood, or validated? Ask yourself, “How would I behave if this is really what I want?”

Tone down your approach. Open yourself up to the belief that others might have something to say and might hold a piece of the puzzle. Back off your harsh and conclusive language. But don’t back off your belief. Hold to your belief; merely soften your approach.

My Crucial Conversation: Lori A.

Three years ago, my teenage daughter was diagnosed as bipolar. The manic highs and lows are incredibly frightening, and the abyss of depression after a violent episode made me and my husband truly fear for our daughter’s life. During this difficult time, we had the police at our house to diffuse the violence. She stopped going to school. We had her hospitalized. We prayed a lot.

The good news is that I began using my Crucial Conversations skills during her manic highs and lows, and it worked. The Contrasting statement was extremely effective in diffusing her anger and sadness. Later on, after she was level, the STATE My Path skills became a literal lifesaver. I noticed that if I was careful to remove my judgments when I shared my concerns and just state them factually, then encourage her to share her views, she could hear me much easier.

With the help of Crucial Conversations, I was able to maintain a relationship with my daughter during a time in her life when she was hard to reach. Since her diagnosis and treatment, she has truly turned her life around. As we face more challenges ahead, I can and will continue to use these skills. In many ways, I believe you have helped us save her.

Summary: STATE My Path

When you have a tough message to share, or when you’re so convinced of your own rightness that you may push too hard, remember to STATE your path:

  • Share your facts. Start with the least controversial, most persuasive elements from your Path to Action.
  • Tell your story. Explain what you’re beginning to conclude.
  • Ask for others’ paths. Encourage others to share both their facts and their stories.
  • Talk tentatively. State your story as a story—don’t disguise it as a fact.
  • Encourage testing. Make it safe for others to express differing or even opposing views.
