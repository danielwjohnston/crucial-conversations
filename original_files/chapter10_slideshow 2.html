Setting the Stage

"No one can hurt me without my permission."

‚Äî Often attributed to Gandhi

(This chapter is about how we respond to feedback‚Äînot excusing cruelty or blaming those who've been mistreated)

One of the authors once gave a wedding toast, repeatedly calling the bride by the groom's ex-wife's name. Oops.

Critical feedback can be hard to hear. But there's a difference between getting feedback and being feedsmacked.

The Problem: Feedsmacking

What is Feedsmacking?

In the middle of a meeting, an innocent walk down the hallway, or a performance review, someone delivers a verbal wallop that rocks your psychological footing. And for some, life is never the same.

Examples from the study:

  • "You're an evil person. You're a thief. You are scum."
  • "I need warriors, not wimps."
  • "You're so desperate for someone to love that you're settling."

Many people remember harsh feedback word-for-word decades later, as if embroidered onto a mental sampler.

Carmen's Story

A Moment That Lasted Decades

Carmen worked in a family business. One day, she asked her uncle for feedback. He took off his glasses, locked his eyes on hers and said:

"You should be more like your sister, Linda."

Linda is petite, flirty, coy, and flattering to men. Carmen is tall, independent, resourceful, and straightforward, working with men as equals.

Even though this happened decades ago, it still sticks in her mind.

Sticks and stones may break bones, but these few words shattered self-confidence, hope, and in some cases, life plans.

Lessons from The Other Side Academy

In Salt Lake City sits a red brick Victorian mansion with something innovative: The Other Side Academy (TOSA).

The Students

120 men and women, arrested an average of 25 times each, running businesses together to overcome a lifetime of self-destruction.

No therapists, counselors, or guards‚Äîjust peers finding a way to be self-reliant.

The Results

The Other Side Movers is the #1 rated moving company in the state.

Customers describe them as "awesome," "super respectful," and "conscientious"‚Äîgiving "10 million stars."

How do deeply broken people work harmoniously together to accomplish results that would be the envy of the finest companies?

"No one got arrested for singing too loud in the church choir."

The Secret: Games

Tuesday and Friday Nights, 7-9pm

Students sit in groups of 20 and give each other unvarnished feedback. Games can be loud. Vocabulary is sometimes raw. A single student can be the focus of a score of colleagues for 15-20 minutes without let up.

Peers present you with evidence that you have acted dishonestly, manipulatively, lazily, selfishly, or arrogantly.

The Fundamental Belief

"Relentless exposure to truth is the best path to empathy, growth, and happiness."

Students learn to "take their game"‚Äîlistening non-defensively. Older students advise:

"Just listen, then put it all in a bag and take it to your pillow tonight. There you can decide what is gold and what is crap."

Marlin Takes His Game

Friday Night at TOSA

Marlin, a 55-year-old former junkie and prison alum, sits as a younger student brings him a full-throated game:

"You're a control freak, Marlin. I'm not an idiot. I have construction experience. I know how to do things, but it doesn't matter if my way would work. It has to be your way! Do you get some kind of thrill out of keeping all of us under your thumb? Why can't you let me do it my way sometimes?"

Marlin's Response: He sits comfortably with his arms and legs relaxed. He looks calmly at the younger student. As the student continues, Marlin's face becomes sad.

When his colleague finishes, Marlin looks down, takes a breath, and says:

"I didn't think about how this felt to you. You're right. That is what I do. I'll fix it."

Three years ago, Marlin feared truth and craved approval.

Today, he craves truth and fears approval.

The Pen: Your Power to Define Worth

Think of your pen as the power to define your worth.

When You Hold Your Pen ‚úçÔ∏è

You get to author the terms: Is your worth intrinsic to you? About how you look? Contingent on achievement? How many people admire you?

Your personal security doesn't come from others' opinions. It comes from an innate sense of your enduring worth.

When You Lose Your Pen üìù

Whoever holds your pen can compose the terms of your well-being.

You no longer simply enjoy praise‚Äîyou need it. From that moment, you are fundamentally insecure.

"If you live by the compliment, you'll die by the criticism." ‚ÄîCornelius Lindsay

We're born with our pen firmly in our grasp. Babies don't fret over others' opinions. But as we grow, without realizing it, we hand our pen to others.

The Feedback Puzzle

A Surprising Discovery

Rank these messages from least to most hurtful:

  1. "You are venomous and poisonous."
  2. "You start conversations in the middle."
  3. "You're an evil person. You're a thief. You are scum."
  4. "When you lose your temper, it can make others feel less respected."
  5. "You need to eliminate your shortcomings."

Take a moment to decide your ranking...

The Feedback Puzzle ‚Äî The Reveal

What we expected vs. what the data showed

Most people assume the pain comes from:

  • Severity of words (some messages "should" hurt more)
  • Harshness of delivery
  • Closeness / authority of the person giving feedback

But the surprise was this:

Reported pain didn't track with content or delivery.
When people handed over the pen, any feedback could feel equally painful.

The biggest variable wasn't the message ‚Äî it was who held your pen in that moment.

Dan's Digest

Key Answer Points:

Why feedback is hard: Feedback is difficult to hear when we've handed our "pen" to others‚Äîwhen we've made our sense of worth and safety dependent on their approval. The content and delivery matters far less than who holds our pen in that moment.

How we give away control: We give control of our self-worth when we equate approval with safety (believing disapproval equals danger), or when we believe others' opinions define our worth. This happens gradually as we seek external validation rather than internal security.

We often remember feedsmacking word-for-word decades later because it hit us when we were fundamentally insecure‚Äîwhen someone else controlled our emotional well-being.

Worth Considering:

  • Two parts: Safety (equating disapproval with danger) and Worth (believing we need others' approval to be worthy)
  • Why do the same words hurt differently depending on who says them?
  • Carmen's uncle's comment stuck for decades not because of what he said, but because she'd given him authority over her worth
  • When we're confident in our worth, even harsh feedback becomes information rather than indictment
  • The transition happens as we fail to update our adult capacity to protect ourselves and care for our own worth

Two Parts of the Pen

The pen is the power to define what we need to secure two fundamental psychological needs:

1. Safety

Perceived physical, social, or material security

The Problem: We equate approval with safety and disapproval with danger, failing to update our capacity to protect ourselves.

"You don't get angry when you're confident. You get angry when you're scared."

2. Worth

A sense of self-respect, self-regard, or self-confidence

The Problem: We live with worry that we aren't worthy. Others' feedback becomes an audit of our worth.

When we surrender our pen, we stop generating feelings of worth and start looking for them.

Two Assumptions That Change Everything

  1. Truth is an absolute good.
    The more truth you know, the better you can navigate life.
  2. Feedback is usually a mixture.
    Your job isn't to swallow it whole ‚Äî it's to sort what's true and discard the rest.

We're retrieving the gold without surrendering worth.

The CURE Framework

Four tools help redirect us inside, rather than outside, to secure our safety and worth:

C

Collect Yourself

Breathe deeply and slowly. Name your feelings. Connect with soothing truths like: "This can't hurt me. I'm safe."

U

Understand

Be curious. Ask questions and ask for examples. Focus on understanding: "Why would a reasonable person say this?"

R

Recover

Ask for a time out. Feelings of control bring feelings of safety. Say: "I need time, and I'll get back to you."

E

Engage

Look for truth, not defensively poking holes. Put it in a bag, sort out the gold. Re-engage and share what you commit to do.

C

Collect Yourself

Physical and Mental Grounding

  • Breathe deeply and slowly ‚Äî reminds you that you are safe. Signals you don't need to be preparing for physical defense.
  • Be mindful of your feelings ‚Äî name them as you feel them. Are you hurt? Scared? Embarrassed? Ashamed?
  • Examine your stories ‚Äî identify and critique the stories that led to your feelings.
  • Connect with soothing truths ‚Äî repeat affirmations like "This can't hurt me. I'm safe." or "If I made a mistake, it doesn't mean I am a mistake."

What to Say (in the moment)

  • "Give me a second ‚Äî I can feel myself getting defensive."
  • "I want to hear this well. Let me take a breath."
  • "Okay. I'm noticing I'm feeling (embarrassed / scared / annoyed). I'm here."

Marlon's Affirmation: "I have infinite, intrinsic, and eternal worth. Neither my past nor others' opinions define me. My worth is about my potential and my choices."

U

Understand

Be Curious, Not Defensive

Curiosity can inoculate you against defensiveness. Focusing on understanding helps interrupt our tendency toward personalizing.

  • Ask questions and ask for examples, and then just listen
  • Solve the puzzle: "Why would a reasonable, rational, decent person say what they're saying?"
  • Detach yourself from what is being said as though it's about a third person
  • Act like a good reporter trying to understand the story

What to Say (stay curious)

  • "Help me understand ‚Äî can you give me an example?"
  • "What did you see that led you to that conclusion?"
  • "What would 'better' look like to you?"

It's hard to beat yourself up when you're busy solving a puzzle.

R

Recover

Give Yourself Permission to Process

It's sometimes best to ask for a time out. Feelings of control bring feelings of safety, and you regain a sense of control when you exercise your right to respond when you're truly ready.

What to Say (take the timeout)

  • "I want to get this right. Can I think about it and circle back?"
  • "I'm not ready to respond well yet ‚Äî give me a little time."
  • "I heard you. I'm going to sit with it, and I will follow up."

At The Other Side Academy

Students sometimes simply say, "I will take a look at that."

They don't agree, they don't disagree. They simply promise to look sincerely at what they were told on their own timeline. They put it in the pool of meaning and let it marinate until they are in full possession of their pens.

E

Engage

Look for the Gold

If you've done a good job re-establishing feelings of safety and worth, you'll look for truth rather than defensively poking holes in the feedback.

Even if it's 95% junk and 5% gold, look for the gold.

There is almost always at least a kernel of truth in what people are telling you. Scour the message until you find it.

What to Say (close the loop)

  • "Here's what I heard‚Ä¶"
  • "Here's what I agree with‚Ä¶"
  • "Here's what I don't see the same way‚Ä¶"
  • "Here's what I'll do next ‚Äî and when you'll see it."

Then Re-Engage

If appropriate, re-engage with the person who shared the feedback and:

  • Acknowledge what you heard
  • Share what you accept
  • Commit to what you will do
  • If needed, share your view of things in a non-combative way

Dan's Digest

Key Answer Points:

Preparation is proactive: The CURE framework is your preparation toolkit. Most people struggle with Collect (staying calm in the moment) or Engage (looking for truth rather than defending). The framework is sequential‚Äîyou can't Understand if you haven't Collected yourself first.

Build your foundation now: Practice affirming truths in advance, like Marlin's affirmation: "I have infinite, intrinsic, and eternal worth. Neither my past nor others' opinions define me. My worth is about my potential and my choices."

Start small: Practice in low-stakes situations (minor feedback from coworkers) before high-stakes situations (spouse, boss, performance reviews).

Worth Considering:

  • Collect is foundational‚Äîwithout it, the other three steps are nearly impossible
  • Understand (curiosity) is the antidote to defensiveness and personalizing
  • Recover gives you permission to not respond immediately‚Äîtime often reveals gold we couldn't see when emotional
  • Engage is where growth happens‚Äîit's easier to poke holes than to look for truth
  • Common tough feedback scenarios: performance reviews, spouse/partner feedback, corrections from your boss, criticism from parents or grown children

Marlin's Transformation

Three Years Ago

Marlin was a 55-year-old former junkie and prison alum. When he arrived at TOSA, he was emotionally brittle. The slightest criticism or suggestion of disapproval sent him into an angry tirade.

He feared truth and craved approval.

Today

Marlin is a foreman with The Other Side Builders. When receiving feedback, he sits comfortably with his arms and legs relaxed, looks calmly, and says: "You're right. That is what I do. I'll fix it."

He craves truth and fears approval.

What Changed?

Over three years, Marlin participated in 300 games. That is a lot of feedback. But what he came to learn is that how he responded to the feedback was more important than the feedback itself.

As he learned to be the steward of his own safety and worth, he cultivated a peace that has changed everything. He is reunited with parents and siblings with whom he had been at war for 30 years.

Discussion Question #3

How do you determine what feedback is valuable?

How do you know what feedback to take to heart?

Take 3 minutes to discuss this with the group

Dan's Digest

Key Answer Points:

"Put it in a bag, take it to your pillow" - TOSA students learn to collect all feedback without immediately judging it. Later, with a calm mind, they sort through it looking for the gold.

Ask: "Why would a reasonable person say this?" This question helps you find truth even in hostile or poorly delivered feedback. Even 5% gold in 95% junk is worth finding.

Patterns matter more than single incidents. One person's opinion might be an outlier. Multiple people noticing the same thing is signal, not noise.

Worth Considering:

  • Truth is an absolute good - more truth means better navigation through life
  • Look for the gold, not defensively poking holes in the feedback
  • The more confident you are in your worth, the more freely you can look for truth
  • Feedback about patterns is more valuable than one-off complaints
  • Your goal is growth, not being right - valuable feedback points to blind spots

Key Takeaways

Your Reaction is Within Your Control

How you experience feedback has more to do with the location of your pen than the content of the message.

You Are the Steward of Your Safety and Worth

When you become an adult, the pen is yours. You are responsible for and capable of caring for yourself.

Truth is an Absolute Good

The more truth you know, the better you can navigate life. Put feedback in a bag, sort what's true, and discard the rest.

The Deeper Issue

Those who acknowledge and address this deeper issue don't just get better at crucial conversations‚Äîthey become better equipped for all of life's vicissitudes.
