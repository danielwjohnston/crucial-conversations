How to Notice When Safety is at Risk

"I have known 1,000 scamps, but I never met one who considered himself so. Self-knowledge isn't so common."
— Ovid

A Failed Crucial Conversation

Let's visit a failed crucial conversation to understand what to look for:

The Scenario:

You and your team have worked hard on a proposal for a company acquisition. Your manager is taking it to the steering committee and has invited you to observe.

You're excited because you believe in your recommendation and want to see leadership in action.

But then something goes wrong...

Think About It:

Have you ever been in a meeting that started well but ended badly? What were the warning signs?

The First Signs of Trouble

As the meeting begins, you notice subtle changes in the room:

Physical Signals:

  • People crossing their arms
  • Leaning back in chairs
  • Avoiding eye contact
  • Tensing up shoulders
  • Forced smiles that don't reach eyes

Verbal Signals:

  • Short, clipped responses
  • Sudden silence
  • Changing the subject abruptly
  • Overly agreeable without engagement

The Three Conditions of Dialogue

When people feel unsafe, they react in predictable ways. Look for these three conditions:

1. Silence

  • Withdrawing
  • Avoiding
  • Hiding information
  • Playing it safe

2. Violence

  • Controlling
  • Labeling
  • Attacking
  • Forcing opinions

3. Self-Justification

  • Making excuses
  • Blaming others
  • Rationalizing behavior

How to Look for Safety Problems

Step 1: Watch for Content and Conditions

Pay attention to both what people are saying AND how they're behaving.

Step 2: Look for Patterns

Is this a one-time reaction or a recurring pattern of behavior?

Step 3: Check Your Own Reactions

Are you feeling defensive? Scared? Angry? Your emotions are valuable data.

Step 4: Notice the Shift

When did the conversation move from dialogue to debate or defense?

Specific Behaviors to Watch For

When People Move to Silence:

  • Withdrawing: Physically or emotionally leaving the conversation
  • Avoiding: Changing topics, making jokes, deflecting
  • Hiding: Withholding information, downplaying concerns

When People Move to Violence:

  • Controlling: Interrupting, monopolizing, forcing decisions
  • Labeling: Name-calling, stereotypes, generalizations
  • Attacking: Belittling, threatening, condemning

These are not character flaws—they're safety responses.

The Early Warning System

The key to crucial conversations is catching problems early:

Level 1: Subtle Signs

Physical tension, forced agreement, topic changes

Level 2: Overt Behaviors

Clear silence patterns, controlling language, defensive responses

Level 3: Crisis Mode

People shutting down completely, open hostility, relationship damage

The Goal:

Catch problems at Level 1 or 2, before they reach Level 3.

Early detection = easier intervention
