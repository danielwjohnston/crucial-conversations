Core Message

The sooner you notice you're not in dialogue, the easier it is to get back—and the lower the costs.

When stakes get high, we get caught up in what we're saying. We must also observe the process—how it's being said.

Learn to Dual Process

The key to maintaining dialogue

Content

What is being said

The topic, the facts, the argument

Process

How it's being said

Safety, emotions, behaviors

Challenge: When stakes get high, key brain functions shut down. We become so consumed with our ideas that we lose track of what we're doing.

Watch for Three Conditions

What to look for during crucial conversations

  • 1. The moment a conversation turns crucial
    Physical signs, emotions, or behaviors that signal danger
  • 2. Signs that people don't feel safe
    Silence or violence emerging in the conversation
  • 3. Your own style under stress
    How YOU behave when feeling unsafe

Learn to Spot Crucial Conversations

Stay alert for warning signs

Physical Signals

Stomach gets tight, eyes dry, heart races, palms sweat

Emotional Signals

Feeling scared, hurt, defensive, or angry

Behavioral Signals

Raising voice, pointing finger, becoming very quiet

What are YOUR cues? Learn to recognize them as signs to step back and slow down.

Learn to Look for Safety Problems

When it's safe, you can say anything

Dialogue calls for the free flow of meaning—and nothing kills meaning like fear.

  • When you fear people aren't buying your ideas → you push too hard
  • When you fear you may be harmed → you withdraw and hide
  • Both reactions (fight and flight) are motivated by fear
Critical insight: People rarely become defensive because of WHAT you're saying. They become defensive when they no longer feel safe.

Safety Isn't Synonymous with Comfort

Crucial conversations are by definition hard. We have to stretch, venture into new territory, and feel vulnerable.

The measure of safety: Is meaning flowing? Can I and others share our meaning, have it heard, and listen honestly and respectfully?

When It's Unsafe, You Start to Go Blind

  • Emotions crank up → key brain functions shut down
  • When genuinely threatened, peripheral vision narrows
  • You can scarcely see beyond what's right in front of you

The Solution

Pull yourself out of the content and look for signs that safety is at risk. When you do:

  • You re-engage your brain
  • Your full vision returns
  • Higher reasoning centers stay active

Don't Let Safety Problems Lead You Astray

The wrong response

When others feel unsafe, they attack, make fun of you, insult you, or steamroll you.

Natural tendency: Take the attack at face value and respond in kind (or try to escape)

The right response

Think: "They're feeling unsafe. I need to do something to make it safer."

Be curious instead of angry or frightened

Silence and Violence

Two unhealthy paths when people feel unsafe

Silence

Withholding meaning from the pool

Done to avoid potential problems; always restricts flow of meaning

Violence

Forcing meaning into the pool

Trying to convince, control, or compel others to your point of view

Three Forms of Silence

1. Masking

Understating or selectively showing true opinions

Examples: Sarcasm, sugarcoating, couching

"I think your idea is brilliant... I just worry others won't catch the subtle nuances."

2. Avoiding

Steering completely away from sensitive subjects

"Speaking of cost-cutting, what if we diluted the coffee?"

3. Withdrawing

Pulling out of conversation entirely

"Excuse me, I've got to take this call..."

Three Forms of Violence

1. Controlling

Coercing others to your way of thinking

Tactics: Forcing views, dominating, interrupting, overstating facts, speaking in absolutes, changing subjects

"There's not a person in the world who hasn't bought one of these."

2. Labeling

Dismissing people or ideas with stereotypes

"Only a [political party] would think that's a good idea."

3. Attacking

Making the other person suffer

Tactics: Belittling, threatening

"Try that stupid stunt and see what happens."

Learning to Look Virtually

Expand your data stream

Most virtual communication severely limits our data stream (no body language, tone, or eye contact).

Choose the Right Medium

Best to worst bandwidth:

  • Face-to-face (ideal)
  • Video conferencing
  • Phone call
  • Email, text, instant messaging

When Virtual, Ask for More Data

"I haven't heard back in a couple days. How are you feeling about the proposal?"

"I wish I could see your face. How are you hearing my message?"

Practice This Week

In your next crucial conversation:

  • Watch for your cues: What signals tell you it's getting crucial?
  • Dual process: Pay attention to content AND conditions
  • Look for safety problems: Are people going to silence or violence?
  • Be curious: When you notice unsafe behavior, ask yourself "Why might they feel unsafe?"

Key Takeaway

You can't fix what you don't see

Learn to look for the moment conversations turn crucial, for signs that safety is at risk, and for your own style under stress.

The sooner you notice, the easier it is to restore dialogue—and the lower the costs.
